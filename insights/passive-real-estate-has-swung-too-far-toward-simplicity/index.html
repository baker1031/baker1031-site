Investing Memo · NNN vs. DST

Passive Real Estate Has Swung Too Far Toward Simplicity

A single-tenant net-lease building feels like a bond with bricks. Underneath, it is a single point of failure dressed in corporate clothing. Why structure beats familiarity.

Gerald F. “Jerry” Baker, IIIFounder & Managing Principal · May 5, 2026

The illusion of the simple solution

There is a gravitational pull in investing toward things that feel uncomplicated. A single-tenant, triple-net-leased building is about as close to a simple real estate investment as one can get. A tenant — often a corporate chain you recognize — signs a long lease, pays rent, and handles taxes, insurance, and maintenance. The owner collects a check and goes about their life. It has the feel of a bond with bricks.

This is precisely why the NNN property has become the default landing spot for 1031 exchange investors flush with proceeds from a sold apartment building. The logic is tidy: you need to deploy capital quickly, you want passive income, and the NNN property looks, smells, and tastes like a passive investment.

But in markets — as in physics — the things that appear frictionless rarely are. The investor who stops thinking at the first-level answer almost always pays for the privilege later. The Delaware Statutory Trust has quietly become a more sophisticated alternative to the NNN acquisition, not because it is flashier, but because it handles risk in a structurally different way.

What you are actually buying

You are not just buying yield. You are buying a lender relationship. This is the point most NNN buyers miss entirely.

When you purchase a freestanding NNN property — a pharmacy, a fast-food pad, a dollar store — you are acquiring a loan as much as a building. In almost every NNN acquisition at scale there is mortgage debt attached: typically 50–65% loan-to-value, underwritten against the current tenant’s creditworthiness and the current lease terms.

The first-level thinker says: ‘Great, the tenant has an investment-grade corporate guarantee.’ The second-level thinker asks: ‘What happens at lease expiration? What happens if the tenant’s credit deteriorates? And what happens to my loan when either of those occurs?’

A DST, by contrast, pools investor capital across multiple properties and uses institutional-quality financing arranged at the trust level by the sponsor. The individual investor does not take on the lender relationship directly. Diversification across properties — sometimes a dozen or more — means no single lease expiration, tenant bankruptcy, or dark-store situation can undo the entire investment.

The NNN is a single point of failure dressed in corporate clothing

There is a particular cognitive error that arises when investors confuse a recognizable brand with a durable credit. Nationally recognized tenants have declared bankruptcy at a rate that should, by now, make every commercial real estate investor pause: Bed Bath & Beyond, Tuesday Morning, Rite Aid, Pier 1, casual-dining operators coast to coast.

In each case, investors in single-tenant NNN properties faced the same brutal arithmetic: the tenant vacated, the rent stopped, and the loan remained. A DST portfolio spread across industrial, multifamily, self-storage, and retail does not eliminate this risk — but it distributes it widely enough that one operator’s failure is not a portfolio-level event.

Time is not your friend in a 1031 exchange — and NNN sellers know it

The 1031 exchange creates a time constraint almost perfectly designed to produce poor decisions: 45 days to identify, 180 days to close. Sellers of NNN assets know 1031 buyers are motivated, constrained, and often anchored to the notion that any closing beats a failed exchange.

The DST solves this structurally. Because offerings are pre-assembled and pre-financed, an investor can often identify and close on a DST interest in a fraction of the time required to source, negotiate, and finance a standalone NNN acquisition. The velocity advantage reduces the pressure that produces suboptimal choices.

The passive-management assumption is imperfect on the NNN side

The triple-net lease is marketed as passive. In practice it is low-maintenance, which is not the same thing. NNN owners still manage the lender relationship, field ‘structural’ calls outside the tenant’s responsibility, navigate renewal negotiations years in advance, and handle disposition. None of this is demanding. But it is not zero. A DST investor holds a beneficial interest in a trust; the sponsor manages everything; there is no landlord relationship whatsoever.

The risk assessment: what could go wrong

No instrument is without its failure modes. The DST has meaningful risks that deserve direct acknowledgment.

  • Illiquidity. DST interests are not freely tradeable. The secondary market is thin and pricing can be unfavorable to sellers. An NNN property, whatever its flaws, can at least be listed for sale.
  • Sponsor concentration risk. In a DST you are trusting a sponsor, not just a property. Weak underwriting, excessive fees, or operational deficiencies flow through to the investor.
  • The seven deadly sins of DST structure. Revenue Ruling 2004-86 imposes strict prohibitions: no new financing after closing, no new or modified leases without approval, no capital expenditures beyond normal maintenance. These can become binding in a deteriorating situation.
  • Fees. DST programs carry upfront loads and ongoing fees — visible if you read the PPM carefully, invisible if you don’t.
  • Asymmetry of risk on the downside. In a leveraged DST, a significant value decline can wipe out equity at the trust level, and individual investors have no flexibility to negotiate with the lender directly.

What the market is currently doing

The 1031 market has tilted heavily toward NNN acquisitions because cap-rate compression made NNN yields look comparable to DST yields while buyers felt they were getting something ‘real’ — a deed in their name.

But compression in single-tenant retail has been severe; cap rates in primary markets pushed below 4.5% for investment-grade tenants at the trough. At those prices the yield advantage of a recognizable-brand building is almost entirely hypothetical. Meanwhile, DST offerings in industrial, multifamily, and necessity-retail have continued to offer cash-on-cash yields in the 5–7% range with institutional sponsorship and built-in diversification. The spread between perceived safety and actual structural soundness has rarely been wider.

The philosophy the investor should adopt

Markets, like pendulums, overshoot in both directions. The thoughtful 1031 investor approaches the decision the way a careful architect approaches a foundation: not ‘what is the most popular choice?’ but ‘what happens to this structure under stress?’

The NNN building answers narrowly — its resilience is a function of one tenant, one lease, one lender, one location. The DST answers more broadly — many assets, many tenants, institutional sponsorship, pre-arranged financing. Neither is perfect; both require careful diligence. But the investor who mistakes simplicity for safety is making the same error investors always make at this point in the cycle: confusing familiarity with durability.

The 180-day clock creates urgency. Urgency is the enemy of clarity. Choose structure over familiarity. That is not a revolutionary idea. It is simply good judgment applied consistently.
