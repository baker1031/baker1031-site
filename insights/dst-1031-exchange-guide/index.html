DST 1031 Exchange: Complete 2026 Investor's Guide

A practical guide to using a Delaware Statutory Trust for your 1031 exchange: property types, debt matching, zero-coupon structures, identification rules, and a selection checklist.

Gerald F. “Jerry” Baker, IIIFounder & Managing Principal · May 5, 2026

A Delaware Statutory Trust (DST) is a legally recognized investment vehicle that allows individual investors to own fractional interests in institutional-grade real estate. For 1031 exchange purposes, the IRS recognizes DST interests as ‘like-kind’ property, enabling investors to defer capital gains taxes while enjoying passive income and professional management.

What is a Delaware Statutory Trust (DST)?

A DST is a separate legal entity created under Delaware law to hold title to one or more income-producing real estate assets. Unlike a partnership or direct ownership, the DST holds the deed and investors hold a beneficial interest in the trust itself. IRS Revenue Ruling 2004-86 officially sanctioned the DST as a valid replacement property for a Section 1031 exchange.

When you invest in a DST, you pool capital with other investors to acquire high-value assets — $50 million apartment complexes, large industrial distribution centers, multi-tenant retail hubs — that would be out of reach individually. A ‘Sponsor’ (an institutional real estate firm) handles all day-to-day operations, from leasing and maintenance to eventual disposition.

How do DSTs work for 1031 exchanges?

The primary appeal is the ability to transition from active management — the toilets, tenants, and trash of direct ownership — into a passive investment. The process follows the same federal guidelines as any other exchange:

  1. Sell the relinquished property through a Qualified Intermediary (QI).
  2. Identify replacement property within 45 days of the sale.
  3. Complete the purchase within 180 days, with the QI using your exchange proceeds to acquire your fractional interest in the DST.

Because DSTs are pre-packaged and already financed, they are often used as a backup plan or a solution for investors who cannot find a suitable sole-ownership property within the 45-day window.

What types of properties are available in a DST?

  • Multifamily apartments. Class A/B communities in high-growth markets, diversified across hundreds of units.
  • Industrial and logistics. Warehouses and distribution centers leased to major corporations, benefiting from e-commerce demand.
  • Necessity retail. Centers anchored by grocery or essential services that tend to be resilient in downturns.
  • Medical office buildings. Leased to healthcare systems or specialized practices, offering long-term stability.
  • Self-storage facilities. Efficient assets with low overhead and durable demand.

Understanding debt and leverage in DST investments

A 1031 exchange generally requires replacing the debt on the relinquished property. If you sold with a $500,000 mortgage, you typically must take on $500,000 of debt on the replacement to avoid taxable boot.

DSTs are structured with non-recourse debt already in place — the investor is not personally liable; the lender’s only recourse is the property itself. The debt-to-equity ratio is fixed: if a DST carries 50% leverage and you invest $100,000 of equity, you are credited with $200,000 of total property value, helping you meet your debt-replacement requirement.

What is a zero-coupon DST?

A zero-coupon DST is designed for investors who need to replace a very high amount of debt. 100% of rental income is directed toward paying down the mortgage. The investor receives no monthly cash flow but benefits from principal paydown and is credited with the full debt amount for tax purposes — useful for owners of highly leveraged properties who need to satisfy the debt-replacement rule without needing income.

Identifying DSTs with a Qualified Intermediary

The QI holds your funds in escrow and facilitates paperwork. By the 45th day you submit a written identification form. You generally use one of two rules:

  1. The 3-property rule. Identify up to three properties of any value.
  2. The 200% rule. Identify any number of properties as long as combined fair market value does not exceed 200% of the property you sold.

Most DST investors use the 200% rule because it allows identification of multiple DSTs across sectors and sponsors, creating a diversified portfolio.

Checklist for evaluating DST replacement properties

  • Sponsor track record. How many DSTs taken full cycle? What was the average return?
  • Market fundamentals. Is the property in a landlord-friendly state with growing population and jobs?
  • Lease terms. Are leases NNN? What is the weighted average lease term (WALT)?
  • Debt structure. Is loan maturity aligned with the projected hold? Fixed- or floating-rate?
  • Fee transparency. Are acquisition, management, and disposition fees clearly outlined in the PPM?

The ‘seven deadly sins’ of DSTs

DSTs operate under strict IRS constraints. The trustee cannot:

  1. Accept new capital contributions after the offering closes.
  2. Renegotiate existing debt or borrow new funds.
  3. Reinvest proceeds from the sale of real estate.
  4. Make more than minor, non-structural modifications to the property.
  5. Renegotiate leases or enter new leases (unless a master lease is used).
  6. Retain cash other than necessary reserves.
  7. Fail to distribute all income (other than reserves) to beneficiaries.

These limitations keep the DST ‘passive’ for tax purposes. If a property requires significant redevelopment or a change in use, a DST may not be the appropriate vehicle.

Key takeaways for 2026 investors

  • A DST provides fractional ownership in institutional real estate that qualifies for 1031 deferral.
  • DSTs remove the management burden while providing potential monthly distributions.
  • Non-recourse debt in a DST helps meet 1031 requirements without personal liability.
  • Consider multiple properties and sponsors to mitigate risk.
  • The 45-day identification period is strict; start the review process early.
