Deferred, Then Gone: 721 DST Exchanges, Estate Planning, and Retirement

The 1031-into-DST-into-UPREIT path can reposition a concentrated, management-heavy asset into a passive institutional portfolio — and, held through death, extinguish decades of embedded gain.

Gerald F. “Jerry” Baker, IIIFounder & Managing Principal · May 5, 2026

History doesn’t repeat, but it does rhyme with capital gains

In 1921, Congress enacted the precursor to what became Section 1031 — a recognition that taxing the exchange of like-kind assets was, in effect, taxing the act of thinking rather than the act of earning. A farmer who traded one field for a more productive one hadn’t realized wealth; he had repositioned it. The tax would come when he finally harvested the gains in cash.

That foundational logic has held for over a century. Yet most investors who have spent decades building equity still approach exit as if the only options are a taxable sale or a 1031 into another operational headache. The 721 exchange, paired with a DST as an intermediary vehicle, offers a third path. It is not new — but it remains, stubbornly, underutilized.

What the 721 exchange actually does — and why the DST is the bridge

A 721 exchange, formally an ‘UPREIT contribution,’ allows a property owner to contribute appreciated real estate into a REIT’s operating partnership in exchange for Operating Partnership Units (OP Units) — without triggering immediate capital gains tax. The logic mirrors 1031: no cash changes hands, so no tax event is deemed to occur.

The wrinkle is that most large REIT operating partnerships won’t accept a single investor’s strip mall or apartment building directly. This is where the Delaware Statutory Trust enters as the structural bridge.

Step 1 — 1031 into a DST

Investor sells appreciated property and executes a standard 1031 exchange into a DST — a qualified replacement property that preserves deferral and removes management burden immediately.

Step 2 — DST seasoning

The investor holds the DST interest, typically 2–5 years. This seasoning satisfies IRC requirements and establishes the property’s character as a held investment, not inventory.

Step 3 — 721 contribution

The DST sponsor contributes the underlying properties into an affiliated REIT operating partnership. DST interests convert to OP Units on a proportional basis — tax-free, per IRC §721.

Step 4 — OP Units and beyond

The investor now holds OP Units in an institutional REIT. These may convert to publicly traded REIT shares (a taxable event) or be held, receiving distributions and benefiting from diversification indefinitely.

The elegance is architectural. Each step has legitimate independent economic purpose; no step is manufactured solely for tax avoidance. The investor genuinely transitions from an active, concentrated, management-intensive asset to a passive, diversified, professionally managed portfolio — and the tax code recognizes that as a repositioning, not a liquidation.

The tax tail should never wag the investment dog. But when the tax tail and the investment logic point in the same direction, the disciplined investor pays close attention.

— Jerry Baker, Founder, Baker 1031 Investments

Why this structure deserves serious consideration

  • Deferral compounding. Aggregated federal, state, and depreciation recapture can approach 35–40% of gain on a long-held property. That is the difference between redeploying $1 and redeploying $0.60. Compounded over a decade, the retained capital earns returns on the full dollar.
  • Concentration risk. Thirty years of equity in a single market, asset class, or tenant relationship is a form of risk rarely priced into return expectations. Diversification into an institutional portfolio is not a surrender of return — it is a repricing of risk that most concentrated holders have been carrying for free.
  • Management burden. Time has value. Liability has value. The 11 p.m. phone call has value. The implicit return on recaptured hours is real, even if it never appears on a spreadsheet.

Deferred, then gone: how the 721 DST interacts with the step-up in basis

Of all the advantages embedded in the 721 DST path, the most underappreciated benefits people who are no longer alive to use it. Under current law, assets held at death receive a step-up in cost basis to fair market value at the date of death. A lifetime of deferred gains — accumulated through 1031s, DST holdings, and UPREIT contributions — may effectively disappear for the investor’s heirs.

Consider a commercial property purchased in 1985 for $400,000, exchanged forward through multiple 1031s, ultimately held as OP Units worth $4 million at death — a $3.6 million embedded gain. Sold at any point, that gain is taxable. Held through death, the heirs inherit at a $4 million basis and the gain is gone entirely.

This is not a loophole. It is the designed interaction of two long-standing provisions — deferral under §721 and the stepped-up basis rules under §1014. The structure does not create the step-up; it preserves the deferral long enough for the step-up to matter.

How estate planners use this structure

Estate attorneys and planners increasingly view the 721 DST path not as a tax strategy but as a wealth-transfer strategy. The investor exits active management without triggering gain; OP Units are easier to transfer, gift, or hold in trust than direct real estate; professional management protects the asset through estate administration; and the step-up resets the clock for the next generation.

  • DST and OP Units in trust structures. Both can be held within revocable living trusts, irrevocable trusts, and certain charitable vehicles — far more tractable than fractional interests in direct real estate at death.
  • Charitable remainder trusts (CRTs). Some investors contribute DST interests or OP Units into a CRT, which sells tax-free, reinvests, pays income for life, and passes the remainder to charity.
  • Annual exclusion and lifetime gifts. OP Units can be gifted using annual-exclusion gifts or applied against the lifetime exemption, removing future appreciation from the taxable estate while keeping the asset productive.
  • Portability and QTIP structures. OP Units in a QTIP trust can provide income to a surviving spouse while preserving estate-tax benefits; the unlimited marital deduction defers estate tax further.

The step-up in basis is not guaranteed forever. It has been proposed for elimination in nearly every major tax-reform discussion of the past two decades. The investor who relies on it as a permanent feature of the code is making a political prediction, not an investment decision.

— Jerry Baker, Founder, Baker 1031 Investments

From operator to income recipient: the 721 DST as a retirement income engine

Most investors approaching retirement face the same problem: their wealth is productive but their lifestyle is not. The property generates income — and calls about broken HVAC, lease disputes, and tax appeals. The 721 DST path turns a job into an investment. REIT distributions, typically paid quarterly, replace the net operating income the investor previously managed to collect, without the operational friction.

REIT operating partnerships are required to distribute at least 90% of taxable income to unit holders annually. That structural obligation provides income predictability genuinely different from direct real estate, where vacancy, capex, and lease timing create lumpy cash flows.

  • Distributions. OP Unit distributions from well-capitalized REITs have historically ranged from 4% to 6% annually.
  • Return of capital. REIT distributions carry mixed tax character — a portion may be return of capital (not immediately taxable, but reduces basis), qualified dividend income, or ordinary income.
  • RMD flexibility. OP Units held outside retirement accounts do not trigger Required Minimum Distributions, giving retirees flexibility in managing taxable income.
  • Social Security thresholds. REIT distributions count toward income for Social Security benefit taxation; retirees sensitive to this threshold should model the combined impact.

Who this structure is actually built for

  • The retiring landlord. Ages 60–75, owns 1–4 properties with substantial embedded gain, wants out of active management but can’t afford the tax bill of a straight sale, willing to hold 5+ years.
  • The estate-conscious owner. Primary goal is wealth transfer; less focused on current income than passing appreciated assets with minimal tax erosion.
  • The income-needs retiree. Direct income has become irregular or management-intensive; values the predictability of REIT distributions.
  • The control-oriented operator. Derives satisfaction from direct control. The 721 path requires surrendering decision-making authority — a psychological cost that is real and often underestimated.

What could go wrong — and the asymmetry worth understanding

  • Legislative risk. Congress can and does change the code. §1031 was narrowed in 2017; the step-up under §1014 has been specifically targeted in recent reform proposals. This is the single largest systemic risk to the structure.
  • Structural execution risk. The quality of the sponsor, the underlying underwriting, the specific REIT, and the OP Unit terms matter enormously. Poor execution can trap capital in illiquid, underperforming vehicles.
  • The future 721 is never guaranteed. Marketing language implying a 721 conversion is the ‘natural conclusion’ is not a contractual requirement. If the affiliated REIT does not execute the contribution, the investor may hold a DST interest indefinitely.
  • Retirement-income interruption risk. REIT distributions are not guaranteed; in 2008 and 2020 many REITs reduced or suspended them. A plan built on distributions without adequate reserves can face a liquidity gap at the worst time.
  • Longevity and inflation risk. A fixed or slowly growing distribution may not keep pace with inflation over a 25–30 year retirement; unlike direct ownership, the investor cannot raise rents unilaterally.
  • Fee drag at multiple layers. DSTs carry fees; REIT operating partnerships carry fees; broker-dealers earn commissions. The deferral benefit must be large enough to absorb the drag and still produce superior after-tax, after-fee outcomes. In many cases it is. In some, it is not.

What should guide your decision

Investment structures are neither good nor bad in the abstract — they are appropriate or inappropriate for specific investors in specific circumstances. The 721 DST path is most defensible for the investor with substantial embedded gain in actively managed real estate, a genuine desire to shed operational responsibility, a long time horizon, estate-planning objectives, and the patience to navigate a multi-year, multi-step process with imperfect information.

For that investor — particularly one entering retirement with heirs in mind — the 721 DST is not a tax trick. It is a rational repositioning of a concentrated, illiquid, management-intensive asset into a diversified, institutional, passive structure that generates income, preserves deferral, and may extinguish decades of embedded gain at the moment of death. The tax code, in this instance, is functioning as designed.
