1031 Exchange Mineral Rights: A Complete Guide

Mineral rights and royalties are real property interests that can qualify for a 1031 exchange. How oil, gas, and royalty interests fit into a diversified, tax-deferred strategy.

Gerald F. “Jerry” Baker, IIIFounder & Managing Principal · May 4, 2026

Mineral rights and royalties are real property interests that can qualify for tax-deferred 1031 exchanges. Investors can exchange traditional real estate for oil, gas, or mineral interests, allowing for diversification and passive income while deferring capital gains taxes under Section 1031.

What are mineral rights and royalties in a 1031 exchange?

Mineral rights represent ownership of the natural resources — oil, gas, gold, silver — beneath the surface. In the U.S., mineral ownership can be severed from the surface, creating a separate ‘mineral estate.’ A royalty interest entitles the owner to a portion of production revenue, free of the costs of production. Because the IRS classifies both perpetual mineral rights and royalty interests as real property, they can be eligible for a 1031 exchange.

Understanding the mineral estate

  • Fee simple estate. Ownership of both the surface and everything beneath it.
  • Severed mineral estate. Ownership of the underground resources only.
  • Royalty interest. A non-operating interest providing a share of production revenue.
  • Overriding royalty interest (ORRI). A royalty carved out of the working interest rather than the mineral estate.
  • Leasehold interest. Rights granted to an operator to explore and produce for a set period.

How does Section 1031 apply to oil and gas?

Section 1031 defers capital gains on the exchange of like-kind property held for investment or business use, and the definition of like-kind real property is broad. Perpetual mineral interests, royalty interests, and certain working interests are typically eligible. Investors often use this to transition from active, management-intensive real estate into passive, income-producing mineral interests.

Mineral rights vs. operating interests

  1. Passive vs. active. Royalties are passive — the owner receives a check without paying for drilling, equipment, or labor. Operating interests share in costs and liabilities.
  2. Liability exposure. Royalty owners generally have no liability for environmental issues; operating interest owners carry significant risk.
  3. 1031 compatibility. Working interests can carry ‘dealer’ status or other complexities; royalties are more straightforward like-kind replacements.
  4. Cash-flow stability. Royalties are paid off the top of gross revenue; operating interests see profit only after expenses.
  5. Capital requirements. Royalties require no ongoing capital calls; operating interests may require frequent funding of new wells.

Key benefits of investing in mineral royalties

  • Passive income potential without the toilets, tenants, and trash of traditional real estate.
  • Diversification — mineral assets often have low correlation with traditional real estate markets.
  • Depletion allowance — the IRS allows a depletion deduction (often 15% of gross income) that can shield part of the cash flow.
  • No property management — no structures, insurance, or property managers.
  • Inflation hedge — royalty payments typically rise with energy prices.

The role of DSTs in mineral exchanges

Many investors access mineral rights through a Delaware Statutory Trust. A DST lets multiple investors own a fractional interest in a large, diversified portfolio of mineral royalties — a 1031-compatible, turn-key solution for those who want the benefits of mineral ownership without sourcing, vetting, and managing individual mineral deeds.

Important questions to ask before you exchange

  • Is the interest perpetual? A 1031 generally requires a perpetual interest rather than a term interest.
  • What is the operator’s track record? Royalty value depends heavily on the company doing the drilling.
  • Is the acreage in a core basin? Location matters — basins like the Permian or Bakken offer established infrastructure and proven geology.
  • How are taxes handled? Mineral income may be subject to severance and out-of-state income taxes.

Is a mineral-rights 1031 exchange right for you?

A 1031 into mineral rights can diversify a portfolio, increase passive income, and move away from active management while preserving tax-deferred status. But the complexity of mineral law and the volatility of commodity prices require a disciplined approach. Key takeaways: mineral royalties can be like-kind property for 1031; royalties (unlike working interests) require no capital contributions or operational management; energy assets provide a hedge against inflation and traditional real estate cycles; and expert guidance helps construct a diversified mineral portfolio tailored to your income needs.
