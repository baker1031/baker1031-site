1031 Exchange Identification Rules: The 3-Property, 200%, and 95% Rules

Every 1031 exchange lives or dies by the 45-day identification window. The IRS gives you three ways to identify replacement property — and choosing the right one is what keeps your exchange valid.

Baker 1031 Investments · Educational

When you sell relinquished property in a 1031 exchange, the clock starts immediately. You have 45 calendar days to formally identify your replacement property in writing, and 180 calendar days from the sale to close on it. There are no extensions for weekends or holidays, and the two periods run concurrently — the 180 days are not added on top of the 45.

The identification must be unambiguous: a written document, signed by you, describing each candidate property clearly enough that it can be identified (typically a legal description or street address), and delivered to your Qualified Intermediary (QI) or another party to the exchange (not your own agent) before midnight on Day 45. Once the window closes, you can only acquire property you actually identified.

Because you usually identify more than one candidate to protect against a deal falling through, the IRS limits how many — and how much — you can identify. You may use any one of three rules.

The Three-Property Rule

You may identify up to three properties of any value, and you may acquire one, two, or all three. This is the rule most exchangers use, because it is simple and carries no value cap. The vast majority of straightforward exchanges — selling one property and buying one or two replacements — fit comfortably inside it.

The 200% Rule

If you want to identify more than three properties, you may do so as long as the combined fair market value of everything you identify does not exceed 200% of the total value of the property you sold. In other words, if you relinquished a property worth $2,000,000, you can identify any number of replacement candidates as long as their aggregate value stays at or below $4,000,000. This rule is useful when you are diversifying into several smaller assets, such as a basket of net-lease properties or multiple DST interests.

The 95% Rule

If you blow through both limits — you identify more than three properties and their combined value exceeds 200% of what you sold — your exchange is not automatically disqualified, but you fall under a much stricter requirement. Under the 95% Rule, you must actually acquire at least 95% of the total fair market value of everything you identified. Miss that threshold and the entire exchange can fail. Because the margin for error is so thin, the 95% Rule is rarely used intentionally; it functions mostly as a safety net for investors who over-identified.

How the rules interact

You only need to satisfy one of the three. In practice, exchangers default to the Three-Property Rule, step up to the 200% Rule when they are spreading capital across several assets, and avoid the 95% Rule unless circumstances force it. A single mis-drafted identification letter — too many properties, too much aggregate value, an ambiguous description, or a missed deadline — is one of the most common reasons an otherwise sound exchange collapses.

Where DSTs fit

Delaware Statutory Trusts are frequently used inside the identification rules for two reasons. First, a DST interest is a fractional, pre-packaged real-property interest that can typically close in a few business days, which matters when the 45-day and 180-day clocks are tight. Second, DSTs are a practical backup identification: an exchanger can identify a primary replacement property plus one or more DST interests, and if the primary deal falls apart, the DST can absorb the proceeds and complete the exchange rather than leaving the investor with a taxable boot. Because identifying multiple DSTs can push an exchanger toward the 200% Rule, the aggregate value of all identified property still has to be tracked carefully.

None of this is a substitute for professional advice. The identification rules are unforgiving, and the right structure depends on your specific facts. Work with a Qualified Intermediary and your own tax and legal advisors before you sell, not after.
