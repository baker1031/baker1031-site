How to Choose a Qualified Intermediary

Back to All Insights
1031 Exchange Baker 1031 Research  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  16 min read

Your qualified intermediary holds your entire sale proceeds, often for months, with no comprehensive federal licensing behind them. This complete guide covers how to choose one safely — fund security, controls, bonding, experience, financial strength, fees, red flags, and the exact questions to ask.

The qualified intermediary industry is lightly regulated at the federal level, which means the burden of choosing a safe one falls squarely on you. Since the QI holds your entire sale proceeds — sometimes for the full 180 days — this is one of the most consequential decisions in your exchange, and it should be made on security first and price a distant second. This guide walks through every factor that matters: how funds are held and controlled, the protections to insist on, the experience and financial strength to look for, the fees to understand, the red flags that should make you walk away, and the specific questions that separate a trustworthy QI from a risky one.

Why QI Selection Is So Important

The qualified intermediary sits at the center of your exchange and, for a period of weeks or months, holds the entire proceeds of your sale. If those funds are mishandled, lost, or tied up in a QI's financial failure, you can lose both your exchange and your money — a double catastrophe that no amount of careful identification or replacement-property diligence can undo, as the question of what happens to your funds when a 1031 exchange fails makes painfully clear.

There have been real cases over the years of qualified intermediaries misappropriating client funds or collapsing during financial stress, leaving exchangers without their proceeds and without a completed exchange. These episodes are the exception, not the rule, but they're the reason selection deserves serious attention rather than a quick comparison of fees.

The stakes also explain why fund security should dominate your decision. A few hundred dollars of fee difference is irrelevant next to the safety of hundreds of thousands of dollars of proceeds. Choose the QI you'd trust with your life savings, because for the duration of the exchange, that's effectively what they're holding.

The Lack of Federal Regulation

Unlike banks or broker-dealers, qualified intermediaries are not subject to a comprehensive federal licensing and oversight regime. Regulation varies by state — some states impose bonding, segregated-account, and registration requirements, while others impose little or nothing. There is no single federal regulator standing behind your QI.

This regulatory gap is precisely why due diligence falls on you. You can't assume that a firm calling itself a qualified intermediary meets any particular standard of fund security or financial strength, because in many places no one is checking. The protections you get are the ones you insist on and verify.

The upshot isn't to avoid QIs — they're required, and most are reputable, well-run firms. It's to choose deliberately, favoring established intermediaries with strong, transparent controls and a long track record, rather than picking the cheapest option or whoever your closing agent happens to suggest.

Fund Security: Segregated & Qualified Accounts

The most important protection a QI provides is how it holds your money. Insist on funds held in segregated, qualified accounts — ideally a separate account for each client's exchange — rather than commingled with other clients' funds or, worse, with the QI's own operating capital. Commingling is a serious risk: it can expose your funds to other clients' problems or the QI's creditors.

Ask specifically how accounts are titled, at which bank the funds are held, and whether the QI uses qualified escrow or qualified trust arrangements, which add a layer of legal protection around the funds. A strong QI will answer these questions readily and in detail.

The bank matters too. Funds held at a large, well-capitalized institution are safer than funds at a small or troubled one. You're entitled to know where your money will sit, and a QI that's evasive about it is telling you something important.

Dual Authorization and Controls

Beyond where the funds sit, ask how they move. Dual authorization — requiring two approvals to disburse exchange funds, sometimes including the client's — is a critical control that prevents a single individual at the QI from moving your money improperly. It's one of the strongest protections against internal fraud.

Other controls worth asking about include written disbursement procedures, background checks on employees who handle funds, and independent audits of the firm's operations and accounts. These internal controls are what separate a disciplined operation from one where a single bad actor could do damage.

A QI with robust controls will describe them confidently, because strong firms know that sophisticated clients ask. Vagueness or reluctance about how funds are authorized and moved is a meaningful red flag.

Bonding and Insurance

A strong QI carries a fidelity bond, which protects against employee theft or misappropriation, and errors-and-omissions (E&O) insurance, which covers professional mistakes. Both should be sized to the volume of funds the QI holds, not just a token amount.

Ask for the specific coverage limits and compare them to the QI's total funds under administration and to the size of your own transaction. A bond of a few million dollars offers thin protection at a firm holding hundreds of millions across clients — the coverage should be meaningful relative to the risk.

Bonding and insurance are not a substitute for sound fund handling, but they're an important backstop. Together with segregated accounts and dual authorization, they form a layered defense around your proceeds.

Experience & Track Record

Experience is both a competence and a safety factor. A QI that has handled thousands of exchanges over many years has seen the problems that arise and built processes to prevent them — and has demonstrated staying power through different market conditions.

Ask how long the firm has been in business, how many exchanges it handles annually, and whether it has operated through downturns. Longevity matters: firms that have weathered multiple cycles are less likely to be the ones that fail when markets turn.

Experience with your specific property type and exchange structure also matters. A QI fluent in standard delayed exchanges may be less prepared for a reverse, improvement, or mineral exchange, each of which involves specialized documentation and, sometimes, parking entities.

Financial Strength

Because the QI holds your funds, its own financial health is part of your risk. A financially strong firm with audited financials, a clean balance sheet, and institutional backing is far less likely to fail in a way that imperils your proceeds.

Ask whether the QI will provide audited financial statements and whether it's part of, or backed by, a larger, well-capitalized organization (some of the strongest QIs are affiliated with banks or title companies). Institutional backing can add stability and, sometimes, additional protections for client funds.

Financial strength interacts with how funds are held. Even a financially weak QI poses less risk to properly segregated, qualified-account funds — but the safest combination is a strong firm and sound fund handling, and that's what you should look for.

Reputation and References

A QI's reputation among professionals who work with it repeatedly — real estate attorneys, CPAs, and advisors — is a valuable signal. These professionals see how QIs perform across many transactions and quickly learn which ones are reliable and which cause problems.

Ask the QI for references, and ask your own attorney, CPA, or advisor whom they trust. An independent advisor in particular can often point you to QIs they've worked with successfully across many exchanges, which is a strong endorsement.

Also do basic background research: check for complaints, litigation, or regulatory actions where available. A clean reputation built over years is hard to fake and is one of the better indicators of a QI you can trust.

Fee Transparency

QI fees are modest relative to the tax at stake — typically a base fee for a standard delayed exchange plus per-property charges for additional replacements, with higher fees for reverse and improvement exchanges. The fee itself should be a secondary consideration to security, but transparency about it is a useful signal.

Ask for a clear, written fee schedule, and ask specifically about interest: some QIs earn and retain the interest on the funds they hold (the "float"), while others share or credit it to you, particularly on larger balances. Understanding the full economics — fees plus float — tells you the true cost.

Be wary of a QI that's unusually cheap, especially if it's vague about fund security. Below-market pricing can be a sign of a firm cutting corners or relying on float income in ways that don't align with your interest in safety. Transparency about both fees and how funds are handled is the hallmark of a trustworthy intermediary.

Key Takeaways
  • Choose for fund security first: segregated qualified accounts, dual authorization, bonding, insurance.
  • Favor experienced, financially strong QIs with a long track record and clean reputation.
  • Understand the full economics (fees plus float), and treat unusual cheapness or vagueness as a red flag.

Specialized Experience for Complex Exchanges

If your exchange isn't a standard delayed swap, specialized QI experience becomes essential. Reverse and improvement exchanges require the QI (or an affiliate) to form and operate an exchange accommodation titleholder that parks property under the IRS safe harbor — a more complex structure that not every QI handles well.

Mineral and royalty exchanges involve deeds, division orders, and the routing of trailing royalty checks, which a general QI may not be equipped to handle. DST-heavy exchanges benefit from a QI comfortable coordinating with sponsors and accustomed to the documentation of fractional interests.

If your situation involves any of these, confirm the QI's specific experience with that structure before engaging them. A QI that's excellent at standard exchanges but new to your structure can introduce errors precisely where the stakes and complexity are highest.

Red Flags to Avoid

Certain signs should make you walk away. Reluctance or vagueness about how funds are held, titled, and authorized is the biggest red flag — a trustworthy QI is transparent about all of it. Commingled accounts, or an unwillingness to confirm segregation, are serious warnings.

Other red flags include coverage (bond and E&O) that's small relative to funds held, an unusually short track record, pressure to move quickly without proper documentation, below-market pricing paired with evasiveness about safety, and any history of complaints or regulatory problems.

Trust your diligence over a sales pitch. A QI that resists straightforward questions about fund security, controls, financial strength, and references is telling you something — and with your entire proceeds at stake, you should listen.

Questions to Ask Before Hiring

Approach the selection like an interview for someone who'll hold your life savings. Ask: How are funds held and titled? Are accounts segregated per client, and at which bank? Is dual authorization required to release funds? What are your fidelity bond and E&O coverage amounts, and how do they compare to the funds you hold?

Continue: How long have you been in business, and how many exchanges do you handle annually? Will you provide audited financials and references? How is interest on held funds handled? Do you have experience with my specific exchange type — reverse, improvement, mineral, or DST?

The answers, and the willingness to give them, tell you most of what you need to know. A QI that answers confidently and in detail is one you can likely trust; one that deflects is one to avoid. An independent advisor can help you ask the right questions and interpret the answers.

How an Independent Advisor Helps You Vet a QI

You don't have to vet a qualified intermediary alone, and most investors shouldn't. An independent, sponsor-agnostic advisor who handles exchanges regularly has worked alongside many QIs and has seen firsthand which ones execute cleanly, hold funds securely, and communicate well under deadline pressure — and which ones cause problems, which is part of how to choose a 1031 exchange company across all of its roles. That accumulated experience is hard to replicate from a few phone calls of your own.

A good advisor can shortlist QIs they trust, help you ask the right diligence questions, and interpret the answers — distinguishing, for example, between a firm with genuinely segregated qualified accounts and one using language that sounds protective but isn't. They can also match the QI to your specific exchange, steering you toward one with reverse, improvement, or mineral experience if your situation calls for it.

Crucially, an independent advisor has no incentive to push you toward a particular QI for their own benefit; their interest is in your exchange completing cleanly. That alignment makes their QI recommendations especially valuable, and it's one more reason to assemble your full team — QI, CPA, and advisor — before you sell.

The advisor also coordinates the QI with the rest of the process: ensuring the QI is engaged before closing, that the exchange documents are in order, that funds flow correctly, and that the QI's deadlines and the replacement-property timeline stay aligned. The QI handles custody and mechanics; the advisor makes sure those mechanics serve a well-structured, on-time exchange.

Making the Final Decision

Weigh the factors with security at the top: sound fund handling (segregated qualified accounts, dual authorization), adequate bonding and insurance, genuine experience and financial strength, a clean reputation, transparent fees, and any specialized experience your exchange requires. A QI that scores well across these is a safe choice even if it isn't the cheapest.

Engage your chosen QI before your relinquished property closes — well before, ideally during contract negotiation — so the exchange documents are prepared properly and you've had time to complete your diligence. Rushing the selection because you're already at closing is exactly the situation to avoid.

Finally, lean on your team. Your attorney, CPA, and an independent advisor have likely worked with reputable QIs and can steer you toward firms they trust and away from those they don't. The QI decision is too important to make alone or in a hurry, and the right one quietly underpins the entire exchange.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I choose a qualified intermediary?

Prioritize fund security — segregated, qualified accounts, dual authorization, fidelity bonding, and E&O insurance — then experience, financial strength, reputation, and fee transparency. Ask how funds are held and titled, the coverage amounts, how many exchanges they handle, and whether they'll provide audited financials and references.

Are qualified intermediaries regulated?

There is no comprehensive federal licensing regime for QIs; regulation varies by state, with some imposing bonding and account requirements and others little. Because no single regulator stands behind your QI, due diligence on fund security and financial strength is on you.

What protects my funds with a QI?

Segregated, qualified accounts (ideally one per client), dual-authorization controls on disbursements, fidelity bonding against theft, and errors-and-omissions insurance, ideally backed by a financially strong firm. Confirm these exist and are sized to your transaction before engaging.

Why is fund security more important than the fee?

Because the QI holds your entire proceeds — often hundreds of thousands of dollars — for weeks or months. A small fee difference is irrelevant next to the risk of those funds being lost or tied up in a QI's failure. Choose on safety first.

What is dual authorization?

A control requiring two approvals (sometimes including the client's) to move exchange funds, preventing a single individual at the QI from disbursing your money improperly. It's one of the strongest protections against internal fraud.

How much bonding and insurance should a QI have?

Enough to be meaningful relative to the funds it holds and your transaction size. Ask for the fidelity bond and E&O coverage limits and compare them to the QI's total funds under administration; a small bond at a firm holding hundreds of millions offers thin protection.

Does the QI's financial strength matter?

Yes. Because the QI holds your funds, its own solvency is part of your risk. A financially strong firm with audited financials — and sometimes institutional or bank backing — is far less likely to fail in a way that imperils your proceeds.

What red flags should make me avoid a QI?

Vagueness about how funds are held and authorized, commingled accounts, coverage that's small relative to funds held, a very short track record, pressure to move without documentation, below-market pricing paired with evasiveness, and any history of complaints or regulatory actions.

Should my QI have experience with my exchange type?

For standard delayed exchanges, broad experience suffices. For reverse, improvement, mineral, or DST-heavy exchanges, confirm specific experience, because these involve parking entities or specialized documentation where an inexperienced QI can introduce costly errors.

How do I research a QI's reputation?

Ask the QI for references, ask your attorney, CPA, or advisor whom they trust, and check for complaints, litigation, or regulatory actions where available. Professionals who work with QIs repeatedly are a reliable source of which firms perform and which cause problems.

Who keeps the interest on my exchange funds?

It varies by QI and balance — some retain the interest as compensation, others share or credit it to you. Ask directly, and also ask how and where the funds are held, since safety should come before chasing yield on held funds.

When should I select and engage a QI?

Before your relinquished property closes, ideally during contract negotiation, so the exchange documents are prepared properly and you have time for diligence. Selecting a QI in a rush at closing is exactly the situation that leads to poor choices.

Can my attorney or CPA recommend a QI?

Yes, and they often can — professionals who handle exchanges regularly know which QIs are reliable. An independent advisor can also point you to QIs they've worked with successfully. Just confirm the recommended QI meets the fund-security and experience standards yourself.

Is a bigger or bank-affiliated QI safer?

Often, larger or bank-affiliated qualified intermediaries bring financial strength, institutional controls, and sometimes additional protections for client funds, which can reduce the risk of a QI failure imperiling your proceeds. But size alone isn't a guarantee — a smaller firm with rigorously segregated accounts, dual authorization, strong bonding, and a clean track record can be very safe, while a large firm with weak controls is not automatically secure. Evaluate the actual fund-handling practices, not just the brand or balance sheet.

What is commingling and why is it dangerous?

Commingling is mixing multiple clients' exchange funds, or client funds with the QI's own operating capital, in a single account. It's dangerous because your proceeds can become exposed to other clients' disputes, to the QI's creditors, or to misappropriation that's harder to detect in a pooled account. Insist on segregated, qualified accounts — ideally one per client — so your funds are legally and practically separate from everyone else's.

How do I verify a QI's fund-security claims?

Ask for specifics in writing: how accounts are titled, at which bank funds are held, whether segregation is per client, whether dual authorization is required to disburse, and the exact fidelity bond and E&O coverage amounts. Request audited financials and references, and have your attorney, CPA, or advisor review the answers. A trustworthy QI provides this readily; evasiveness is itself the answer you need.

Does the cheapest qualified intermediary save me money?

Rarely in any way that matters. QI fees are small relative to the tax a 1031 exchange defers, so choosing on price risks trading a few hundred dollars of savings for weaker fund security on hundreds of thousands of dollars of proceeds. Unusually low pricing can also signal corner-cutting or heavy reliance on float income. Choose on safety and competence first; the fee difference is noise by comparison.

Can I switch qualified intermediaries during an exchange?

Switching mid-exchange is difficult and risky once funds are held and contracts are assigned, because the QI is integral to the structure that prevents constructive receipt. The practical answer is to choose carefully before you engage one, since changing QIs after closing can jeopardize the exchange. This is exactly why front-loading the diligence — before the relinquished property sells — matters so much.

What role does the QI play after the exchange closes?

Once the replacement property is acquired and any excess cash (boot) is released to you, the QI's active role largely ends, but it should retain and provide the exchange documentation you and your CPA need for Form 8824 and your records. Keep copies of the exchange agreement, assignments, identification notice, and closing statements yourself as well, in case the exchange is ever questioned.

Glossary

Qualified Intermediary (QI)
An independent party that holds exchange proceeds and documents the transaction; required for a deferred 1031.
Segregated Account
A separate account holding a single client's exchange funds, not commingled with others or the QI's own funds.
Dual Authorization
A control requiring two approvals to move exchange funds, guarding against internal fraud.
Fidelity Bond
Insurance protecting held funds against employee theft or misappropriation.
Errors & Omissions (E&O) Insurance
Coverage for professional mistakes by the QI.
Qualified Escrow / Trust
Arrangements that add a protective legal layer around how exchange funds are held.
Commingling
Mixing multiple clients' funds, or client and operating funds, in one account — a practice to avoid.
Float
Interest earned on exchange funds held during the exchange period.
Audited Financials
Independently examined financial statements that evidence a QI's financial strength.
Exchange Accommodation Titleholder (EAT)
An entity that parks property in reverse and improvement exchanges; requires specialized QI experience.
Track Record
A QI's history and volume of exchanges, a measure of competence and staying power.
Constructive Receipt
Access to or control over proceeds, which disqualifies the exchange and which the QI prevents.
Institutional Backing
Affiliation with a larger, well-capitalized organization (e.g., a bank or title company) that adds stability.
