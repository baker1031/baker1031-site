Opportunity Zone Penalties for Non-Compliance

Back to All Insights
Opportunity Zone Funds Baker 1031 Research  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  16 min read

Qualified Opportunity Funds must keep at least 90% of their assets in qualifying OZ property — and failing this test triggers monthly penalties tied to the IRS underpayment rate. This guide explains how OZ penalties are calculated, the 90% test, investor-level consequences, curing a failure, and why choosing disciplined sponsors matters.

The Opportunity Zone program offers powerful tax benefits, but those benefits come with compliance obligations — and failing to meet them carries penalties. The central requirement is the 90% asset test: a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF) must hold at least 90% of its assets in qualifying OZ property, tested semiannually. When a QOF falls short, it faces a monthly penalty calculated on the shortfall, and persistent failures can jeopardize the fund's status and the tax benefits investors are counting on. For investors, this makes the sponsor's compliance discipline a critical factor — a fund that consistently meets the 90% test (or properly cures and documents any shortfall) protects your benefits, while a sloppy one can put them at risk. This guide explains how OZ penalties are calculated, how the 90% test works, the investor-level consequences, how a failure can be cured, and why choosing disciplined sponsors matters. Note that OZ rules are time-sensitive and evolving, and this is educational information, not tax advice — verify the current rules with your tax advisor.

How penalties are calculated

The core OZ penalty applies at the fund level when a QOF fails the 90% asset test. The penalty for a given month is calculated as the shortfall — the amount by which the fund falls below the 90% threshold of qualifying OZ property — multiplied by the IRS underpayment rate under Internal Revenue Code §6621, applied on a monthly basis. So the penalty scales with both how far short the fund fell and how long the shortfall persists, accruing month by month until cured.

Because the penalty is tied to the §6621 underpayment rate (which the IRS sets quarterly and which moves with interest rates), the cost of non-compliance varies with prevailing rates — and because it accrues monthly, a prolonged shortfall compounds the penalty over time. So the penalty is designed to discourage QOFs from holding non-qualifying assets, charging a rate-based cost for each month and each dollar of shortfall below the 90% threshold.

So the penalty mechanism is a monthly, shortfall-based charge. The 90% test and penalties

The 90% asset test is the requirement the penalty enforces. A QOF must hold at least 90% of its assets in qualifying Opportunity Zone property, and compliance is measured as the average of two testing dates: the last day of the first six months of the fund's taxable year, and the last day of the taxable year. The fund self-certifies and reports its compliance on IRS Form 8996, which is also where any penalty is computed. So the 90% test is the central ongoing compliance obligation of a QOF.

If, on either testing date (and on the average), the fund holds less than 90% in qualifying property, it has a shortfall and owes the penalty for the relevant months — unless it qualifies for relief (discussed below). The test gives funds some flexibility (such as a working-capital safe harbor allowing reasonable time to deploy capital into projects), but the underlying requirement is firm: keep at least 90% in qualifying OZ property, or face the monthly penalty on the shortfall.

Investor-level consequences

While the 90% penalty is charged at the fund level, the consequences flow through to investors. The most immediate effect is that the penalty reduces the fund's assets and returns — a fund paying penalties is leaking value, which ultimately comes out of investors' pockets. But the more serious investor-level risk is that persistent or severe non-compliance can jeopardize the fund's QOF status, and with it the OZ tax benefits investors are counting on (the deferral and the tax-free 10-year appreciation).

If a fund loses its qualification (or an investor's interest fails to be a qualifying investment), the tax benefits can be lost — the deferred gain could be triggered, and the prized 10-year exclusion could be forfeited. So an investor's tax outcome depends on the fund maintaining its compliance over the long hold. This is why a fund's compliance discipline isn't just an administrative detail — it directly protects (or endangers) the benefits that motivated the investment in the first place.

Curing a failure

A QOF that fails the 90% test isn't necessarily doomed — there are mechanisms to address a failure. The penalty itself doesn't apply if the failure is due to reasonable cause — if the fund can show that the shortfall arose despite reasonable care and wasn't due to willful neglect, the §6621-based penalty may be excused for the relevant period. So reasonable-cause relief is the primary safety valve for an inadvertent or unavoidable shortfall.

Beyond reasonable cause, a fund can 'cure' a shortfall by bringing its qualifying-asset percentage back above 90% (deploying capital into qualifying property, disposing of non-qualifying assets, or otherwise correcting the mix) — limiting the penalty to the months it was actually out of compliance. Regulatory safe harbors (like the working-capital safe harbor, which gives funds reasonable time to deploy cash into projects) can also prevent a technical shortfall from triggering a penalty in the first place. So funds have tools to manage and remedy compliance issues.

Choosing disciplined sponsors

Because compliance is central to preserving the benefits, choosing a disciplined sponsor is one of the most important steps an investor can take. A capable, experienced sponsor with strong compliance practices — careful asset management to stay above the 90% threshold, proper use of the working-capital safe harbor, accurate Form 8996 reporting, and good legal and tax counsel — minimizes the risk of penalties and protects the fund's QOF status. So the sponsor's compliance discipline directly safeguards your tax benefits.

Conversely, a weak, inexperienced, or careless sponsor can let the fund slip below 90%, incur penalties, or even jeopardize the fund's qualification — putting investors' benefits at risk. So in evaluating a QOF, look not only at the projects and economics but at the sponsor's compliance track record, the quality of their advisors, and their reporting practices. A sponsor who treats compliance as a priority (not an afterthought) is far more likely to deliver the benefits intact.

Reporting and documentation

Sound reporting and documentation underpin OZ compliance and penalty avoidance. A QOF reports its 90% compliance (and computes any penalty) annually on IRS Form 8996, filed with its tax return — accurate, timely filing is the baseline obligation. Investors who defer gains report their elections on Form 8949 (and related forms), and the fund issues investors the information they need (such as a Schedule K-1 for a partnership QOF). So proper documentation flows from the fund to the investor and to the IRS.

Good documentation also supports reasonable-cause relief and safe-harbor reliance — for example, a fund relying on the working-capital safe harbor should maintain a written plan and schedule for deploying the capital, and document its adherence. Careful records of asset valuations, testing-date measurements, and corrective actions help demonstrate compliance (or reasonable cause) if questioned. So documentation isn't just paperwork; it's the evidence that protects the fund and investors.

What is the working-capital safe harbor?

The working-capital safe harbor is a regulatory provision that lets a QOF (or its operating subsidiary) hold cash and working capital — intended for the acquisition, construction, or substantial improvement of OZ property — for a reasonable period (generally up to a set number of months) without that cash counting against the 90% asset test. To rely on it, the fund must maintain a written plan and schedule identifying how the working capital will be deployed, and substantially comply with that schedule. The safe harbor recognizes that development takes time — a fund raising capital to build can't deploy it instantly, and the safe harbor prevents that undeployed cash from causing a technical 90% failure during the deployment window. So it's an important compliance tool for development-focused QOFs. Proper documentation (the written plan and schedule, and adherence to it) is essential to rely on the safe harbor. Confirm the current parameters and requirements with the fund's counsel and your tax advisor, as the details are technical and subject to change.

The more serious risk is persistent or severe non-compliance that jeopardizes the fund's QOF qualification — that's what could trigger the deferred gain and forfeit the 10-year exclusion. So an occasional, cured shortfall (with a penalty) is different from a fundamental loss of qualification. The distinction matters: penalties reduce returns, but losing qualification can cost the tax benefits entirely. What is the IRS §6621 underpayment rate?

Section 6621 of the Internal Revenue Code sets the interest rates the IRS uses for underpayments (and overpayments) of tax — commonly called the underpayment rate. The IRS determines and publishes these rates quarterly, and they move with prevailing interest rates (generally tied to the federal short-term rate plus a margin). In the OZ context, the §6621 underpayment rate is the multiplier used to compute the monthly penalty when a QOF fails the 90% asset test — the shortfall is multiplied by this rate to determine the penalty for each month of non-compliance. So the §6621 rate makes the penalty interest-rate-sensitive: when underpayment rates are higher, the penalty for a given shortfall is larger. Because the rate changes quarterly, the precise penalty depends on the rates in effect during the months of non-compliance. So the §6621 rate is the cost-of-money benchmark embedded in the QOF penalty. Confirm the current rate with your tax advisor, as it changes regularly and affects the penalty's size.

A fund can address a shortfall in a few ways, though timing matters. Additionally, if the shortfall was due to reasonable cause (despite reasonable care, not willful neglect), the penalty may be excused for the relevant period. And safe harbors (like the working-capital safe harbor) can prevent a technical shortfall from being treated as a failure in the first place. So while a fund can't undo a past testing-date measurement, it can cure the underlying shortfall to stop the penalty and may obtain relief. Do investors pay the QOF penalty directly?

No — the 90% asset test penalty is imposed at the fund level, on the QOF itself, not directly on individual investors. The fund computes and pays the penalty (reported on Form 8996). However, the cost effectively reaches investors indirectly: a fund paying penalties has fewer assets and lower returns, which reduces the value available to investors. And in a pass-through structure, the fund's reduced performance flows through to investors' economic results. More importantly, the real investor-level risk isn't the penalty itself but the possibility that persistent non-compliance jeopardizes the fund's QOF status and the investors' tax benefits. So while you don't write a check for the penalty, you bear its economic effect through reduced returns, and you bear the larger risk to your tax benefits if compliance fails. This is why the fund's compliance discipline matters to you even though the penalty is charged to the fund. Confirm how a specific fund's structure passes through such costs with your advisor.
