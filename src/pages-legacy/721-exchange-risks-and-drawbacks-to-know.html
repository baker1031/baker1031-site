721 Exchange Risks and Drawbacks to Know

Back to All Insights
721 Exchange Baker 1031 Research  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  16 min read

The 721 exchange offers compelling benefits, but it also carries real risks and drawbacks that every investor should understand before proceeding. This guide candidly covers the key risks: the one-way commitment, loss of control, REIT performance and management risk, liquidity and conversion-tax risk, market and valuation risk, and fees and sponsor risk.

The 721 exchange's benefits — deferral, diversification, liquidity, passive income, and estate planning — are genuinely compelling, but a responsible decision requires understanding the risks and drawbacks just as clearly. The 721 exchange is a significant, generally irreversible move into REIT ownership, and it carries risks: you commit to REIT ownership (losing 1031 flexibility), you give up control to the REIT, your returns depend on the REIT's performance and management, accessing liquidity triggers tax, the REIT's value (or share price) can fluctuate, and there are fees and sponsor-related risks. None of these necessarily outweighs the benefits — but you should know them before committing. This guide candidly covers the 721 exchange's risks and drawbacks, so you can make an informed decision with eyes open.

The one-way commitment risk

The most significant risk of a 721 exchange is its one-way nature — it's generally an irreversible commitment to REIT ownership. Once you contribute your property for OP units, you lose 1031 eligibility (OP units aren't real property), so you can't tax-free return to direct real estate. This means you're committing to REIT ownership as a (generally) permanent endpoint, without the ability to reverse the move tax-free.

The risk in this is that your circumstances or preferences might change after the irreversible commitment. If you later wish you'd stayed in direct real estate (to control properties, keep exchanging, or for other reasons), you can't get back tax-free — you'd have to convert your units (triggering the gain) and buy real estate with the after-tax proceeds. So the one-way commitment risks 'locking you in' to REIT ownership even if your preferences change.

This risk is why being certain before committing is essential — because the move is generally permanent, you should be confident REIT ownership is your intended endpoint, not a tentative choice, and clear on who should not do a 721 exchange in the first place. The one-way commitment risk is the foundational drawback, underlying the importance of careful consideration. Understanding this risk underscores the need for certainty before committing. The one-way nature is the defining drawback, making the 721 exchange a decision to approach with full conviction. The irreversibility is the central risk to weigh.

Loss of control

A second key drawback is the loss of control — converting to REIT ownership makes you a passive investor, with the REIT controlling the real estate. As a direct property owner, you control your property: the management, leasing, financing, improvements, and the decisions to hold, sell, or exchange. After a 721 exchange, you hold OP units, and the REIT controls the portfolio — you have no say in the property decisions.

The risk in losing control is that you're now dependent on the REIT's decisions, which may not align with what you'd choose. If the REIT makes decisions you disagree with (acquiring or selling properties, financing choices, distribution levels), you have little recourse — you're a passive unit holder, not a decision-maker. So you've traded control of your own real estate for a passive stake in someone else's decisions.

For owners who value control (making their own real estate decisions), this loss is a significant drawback. For owners ready to step back (wanting passivity), the loss of control is acceptable (even welcome — no more management). So the loss of control is a drawback whose weight depends on how much you value control. REIT performance and management risk

A third risk is REIT performance and management risk — your returns and your holding's value depend on the REIT's performance and management, not your own decisions. After a 721 exchange, your economic outcome is tied to the REIT: its portfolio's performance, its management's decisions, its financial health, and its strategy all determine your returns (distributions) and the value of your units/shares. So you're reliant on the REIT doing well.

The risk is that the REIT may underperform or face problems — poor management decisions, a struggling portfolio, financial difficulties, or strategic missteps can hurt your returns and your holding's value. Unlike a direct property owner who controls their outcome, you're dependent on the REIT's performance, which is outside your control. So a poorly-managed or underperforming REIT directly harms you, with little you can do.

This makes evaluating the REIT critical before converting — since you're tying your outcome to the REIT, its quality, management, track record, and financial health matter greatly. A strong, well-managed REIT reduces this risk; a weak one increases it. The dependence on the REIT's performance is a significant risk, mitigated by careful REIT selection but never eliminated.

Liquidity and conversion-tax risk

A fourth set of risks involves liquidity and the conversion tax. While the 721 exchange offers a path to liquidity (converting OP units to REIT shares), this liquidity has limitations and costs. First, the conversion-tax risk: converting units to shares triggers the deferred gain (taxable), so accessing liquidity beyond the deferral has a tax cost — you can't access your full value tax-free. This means the liquidity is real but taxed.

Second, liquidity limitations: OP units have a holding period before conversion, and the liquidity of the resulting shares depends on the REIT type — a publicly-traded REIT offers robust share liquidity, but a non-traded REIT offers limited (program-based) liquidity. So if you end up in a non-traded REIT, your liquidity may be more constrained than expected. The liquidity isn't guaranteed to be immediate or robust.

These mean the 721's liquidity, while a benefit, comes with the tax cost (conversion triggers gain) and potential limitations (holding period, non-traded constraints). So you shouldn't assume easy, free liquidity — accessing your wealth has a tax cost and may be constrained. Liquidity and conversion-tax risk — the conversion triggering tax (so liquidity isn't free) and liquidity limitations (the holding period, and limited liquidity for non-traded REITs) — are risks to understand about the 721's liquidity. The liquidity is real but taxed and potentially constrained, not easy and free. Understanding these risks tempers the liquidity benefit with realism: accessing your wealth has costs and limits. The liquidity-and-tax risk means the 721's liquidity should be understood as taxed and potentially constrained, not unlimited free access.

Market, share-price, and valuation risk

A fifth risk is market, share-price, and valuation risk — the value of your holding can fluctuate. For a publicly-traded REIT, the REIT's share price (which your units convert into, and which reflects your units' value) fluctuates with the market — so your holding's value can rise and fall with REIT market conditions, interest rates, and investor sentiment. This share-price volatility is a risk: your holding's value isn't stable.

For a non-traded REIT, there's valuation risk — the shares aren't market-priced, so their value is determined by the REIT's periodic valuations (which may be less transparent or timely than market pricing). So you face uncertainty about the true value of a non-traded REIT holding, and the valuations may not reflect real-time conditions. The valuation risk is a different but real concern for non-traded REITs.

Both forms — share-price volatility (traded) and valuation uncertainty (non-traded) — mean your holding's value is subject to market and valuation risk, not fixed. Combined with the broad real estate market risk (the REIT's properties are exposed to real estate cycles), your holding's value can decline. Market, share-price, and valuation risk — share-price volatility (for traded REITs) and valuation uncertainty (for non-traded REITs), plus broad real estate market risk — mean your holding's value can fluctuate and decline. Understanding this risk shows that the 721 exchange doesn't guarantee a stable value; your holding is subject to market and valuation risk. Key Takeaways
  • The one-way commitment risk (generally irreversible loss of 1031 eligibility) is the most significant drawback — it can lock you in.
  • Loss of control (becoming a passive investor) and REIT performance/management risk (your returns depend on the REIT) are key risks.
  • Liquidity has a tax cost (conversion triggers gain) and limitations (holding period, non-traded constraints).
  • Your holding's value is subject to market/share-price risk (traded) or valuation risk (non-traded), plus fees and sponsor risk.

Fees, complexity, and sponsor risk

A final set of drawbacks involves fees, complexity, and sponsor risk. Fees: the 721 exchange and REIT ownership involve costs — the transaction costs, and the REIT's ongoing fees and expenses (management fees, etc.), which reduce your net returns. As with any managed investment, the fees are a drag on returns, and you should understand them. The fee structure varies by REIT and should be evaluated.

Complexity: the 721 exchange is complex — the partnership contribution, the securities nature, the tax mechanics (deferral, basis, conversion, recapture), the documentation, and the ongoing OP-unit considerations. This complexity requires professional guidance (a cost and effort) and means the transaction is harder to understand and execute than a simple sale, opening the door to common mistakes in a 721 UPREIT exchange. The complexity is itself a drawback (requiring expertise and care).

Sponsor risk: you're relying on the REIT's sponsor and management, so the sponsor's integrity, competence, and financial health matter. A problematic sponsor (poor management, conflicts of interest, or financial trouble) is a risk to your investment. Evaluating the sponsor is part of mitigating this risk. Fees, complexity, and sponsor risk — the REIT's fees (reducing returns), the transaction's complexity (requiring professional guidance), and the reliance on the sponsor (whose integrity and competence matter) — are additional drawbacks of the 721 exchange. None necessarily outweighs the benefits, but you should understand them all before committing to this significant, generally irreversible transaction.

Do these risks mean I shouldn't do a 721 exchange?

Not necessarily — the risks are real but must be weighed against the benefits (deferral, diversification, liquidity, passivity, estate planning). For owners ready to commit to REIT ownership (accepting the one-way nature, loss of control, and REIT dependence), the benefits may well outweigh the risks. For owners who value control and flexibility, the risks may be dealbreakers. So the risks inform the decision, they don't automatically disqualify the 721 exchange. Understanding them ensures you decide with eyes open, weighing the real drawbacks against the benefits for your situation.

How can I mitigate the 721 exchange's risks?

Be certain before committing (addressing the one-way risk), choose a strong, well-managed REIT with a sound sponsor (mitigating performance and sponsor risk), prefer a publicly-traded REIT if liquidity matters (addressing liquidity risk), understand the fees, plan conversions to manage the tax, and work with professionals (handling the complexity). Diversifying isn't possible within one conversion, but evaluating the REIT's own diversification helps. So careful REIT selection, certainty, and professional guidance mitigate the risks — though they can't eliminate them. Mitigation reduces but doesn't remove the risks.

Are non-traded REITs riskier in a 721 exchange?

They carry particular risks — more limited liquidity (program-based, not market-traded), valuation uncertainty (periodic appraisals rather than market pricing), and often higher fees. So a non-traded REIT can be riskier on liquidity and valuation than a publicly-traded one. However, non-traded REITs also have potential merits depending on the offering. If liquidity and transparent valuation matter to you, a publicly-traded REIT may be preferable. Understand the specific REIT type's risks — non-traded REITs warrant extra scrutiny on liquidity, valuation, and fees. FINRA has cautioned investors to carefully review non-traded REITs.

Should I get professional advice on the risks?

Yes — given the 721 exchange's significant, generally irreversible nature and its multiple risks (structural, performance, liquidity, market, fees, sponsor), you should assess the risks with professional guidance: a financial advisor (suitability and risk evaluation), your CPA (the tax risks), and your attorney (the structuring and documentation). The complexity and consequences make professional advice essential for understanding and weighing the risks. Don't evaluate the 721 exchange's risks alone — professional guidance ensures you understand them fully and decide soundly. We help you assess the risks candidly as part of the decision.

How do the risks compare to keeping my property?

Keeping your property carries its own risks — concentration risk (your wealth in one property), management burden, illiquidity, and exposure to that property's specific fate (a tenant leaving, local decline). The 721 exchange trades these for different risks (REIT dependence, the one-way commitment, market/valuation risk). So it's not risk versus no-risk; it's one risk profile (concentrated, self-managed direct property) versus another (diversified, passively-held REIT ownership). The 721 reduces concentration risk and management burden but adds REIT-dependence and one-way-commitment risk. Weigh which risk profile fits your situation and comfort better.

Are the risks different for a publicly-traded vs. non-traded REIT?

Yes — a publicly-traded REIT carries share-price volatility (market fluctuations) but offers robust liquidity and transparent pricing. A non-traded REIT carries valuation uncertainty (periodic appraisals rather than market pricing), more limited liquidity (program-based), and often higher fees — but avoids daily market volatility. So the risk profiles differ: traded REITs have market/price risk but liquidity and transparency; non-traded REITs have valuation and liquidity risk but less daily volatility. FINRA has specifically cautioned investors to review non-traded REITs carefully. Consider which risk profile (traded or non-traded) fits your needs when evaluating a 721 exchange.
