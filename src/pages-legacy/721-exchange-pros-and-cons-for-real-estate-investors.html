721 Exchange Pros and Cons for Real Estate Investors

Back to All Insights
721 Exchange Baker 1031 Research  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  16 min read

A 721 exchange offers compelling pros — tax deferral, diversification, liquidity, passive income, and estate planning — but also real cons: the one-way nature, loss of control, the tax on converting units, dependence on the REIT, and fees. This guide gives a balanced look at the pros and cons, who they favor, and how to make the decision.

Like any significant financial strategy, the 721 exchange has both compelling advantages and real drawbacks, and a clear-eyed look at both is essential before deciding. On the pro side, it offers tax deferral (avoiding the substantial tax a sale would trigger), diversification (a stake in the REIT's portfolio instead of one property), liquidity (convertible units), passive income (distributions without management), and powerful estate planning (the step-up and divisibility). On the con side, it's generally a one-way move (you lose 1031 eligibility), you give up control (the REIT manages the portfolio), converting units to shares triggers tax, you depend on the REIT's performance, and there are fees and complexity. Whether the pros outweigh the cons depends on your goals. This guide gives a balanced look at the 721 exchange's pros and cons, who they favor, and how to decide.

The pros

The 721 exchange's pros are substantial and center on five benefits. Tax deferral: under Section 721, contributing property for OP units defers the capital gains tax (the four-layer tax often totaling a third or more of the gain), preserving your full capital. Diversification: your single property becomes a stake in the REIT's diversified portfolio (many properties, types, markets), reducing concentration risk. These two — deferral and diversification — are foundational pros.

Liquidity: OP units are convertible into REIT shares, which (for public REITs) are liquid and tradable, giving a path to liquidity that direct real estate lacks. Passive income: the units pay distributions (comparable to REIT dividends) without any management work, providing hands-off real estate income. So the 721 adds liquidity and passive income to the deferral and diversification — valuable for owners wanting flexibility and hands-off income.

Estate planning: the units receive a step-up at death (erasing the deferred gain for heirs) and divide cleanly among multiple heirs — making the 721 a powerful wealth-transfer tool. This fifth pro is often decisive for older owners. Together, the five pros — deferral, diversification, liquidity, passive income, and estate planning — make the 721 exchange a comprehensive solution. The pros — tax deferral, diversification, liquidity, passive income, and estate planning — are the compelling advantages of the 721 exchange, addressing tax, risk, flexibility, income, and wealth transfer in one transition. Understanding the pros shows why the 721 exchange is attractive for the right owner. These benefits, working together, are the case for the 721 exchange, to be weighed against the cons.

The cons

The 721 exchange's cons are real and must be weighed honestly. The one-way nature: it's generally an irreversible move into REIT ownership — you lose 1031 eligibility (OP units aren't real property), so you can't tax-free return to direct real estate. This is the most significant con for investors who value flexibility. Loss of control: you become a passive investor, with the REIT managing the portfolio; you give up control over the real estate decisions.

The conversion tax: converting OP units to REIT shares (for liquidity) triggers the deferred gain (a taxable event), so accessing liquidity beyond the deferral has a tax cost. REIT dependence: your returns and the value of your holding depend on the REIT's management and performance (and, for public REITs, the share price's market volatility) — you're reliant on the REIT, not your own decisions. So you trade control and self-determination for dependence on the REIT.

Fees and complexity: the 721 exchange involves costs (the transaction, the REIT's fees and expenses) and complexity (the securities nature, the tax mechanics, the documentation), requiring professional guidance. And the investment is subject to the REIT's risks (real estate market, management, etc.). So the cons — one-way nature, loss of control, conversion tax, REIT dependence, fees, and complexity — are meaningful drawbacks. The cons — the one-way nature (lost 1031 eligibility), loss of control, the conversion tax, REIT dependence, and fees/complexity — are the real drawbacks of the 721 exchange, to be weighed against the pros. Understanding the cons honestly is essential to a balanced decision. These drawbacks matter most for investors who value control, flexibility, and self-determination, and they're the case against the 721 exchange for those investors. A clear view of the cons balances the pros.

Weighing pros vs. cons

Weighing the pros against the cons reveals that the 721 exchange's value depends heavily on the investor's goals and priorities. The pros (deferral, diversification, liquidity, passive income, estate planning) align with the goals of an owner ready to exit direct real estate into a diversified, passive, more liquid REIT holding, especially for estate planning. For such an owner, the pros are exactly what they want, and the cons (loss of control, one-way nature) don't bother them (they don't want control or flexibility anymore).

The cons (one-way nature, loss of control, REIT dependence) weigh heavily for an owner who values direct-real-estate control and flexibility — who wants to keep managing and exchanging property, making their own decisions. For such an owner, the cons are significant costs, and the pros (diversification, liquidity, passivity) may not be worth giving up control and flexibility. So the same pros and cons weigh differently depending on the investor.

The balance thus isn't universal — it depends on whether you prioritize the pros (and are okay with the cons) or are deterred by the cons (and don't need the pros). An estate-focused, ready-to-step-back owner finds the pros outweigh the cons; a control-valuing, active investor finds the cons outweigh the pros. Weighing the pros against the cons — recognizing that their balance depends on your goals (the pros favoring owners ready to transition into passive REIT ownership, the cons weighing on those valuing control and flexibility) — is the key to deciding. The 721 exchange isn't universally good or bad; its value depends on whether the pros align with your goals and the cons are acceptable to you. Understanding how to weigh them for your situation is the heart of the decision. The pros-vs-cons balance is individual.

Who the pros favor

The 721 exchange's pros favor specific types of investors. They favor owners ready to exit active real estate — those tired of management, retiring, or wanting to step back, for whom the passivity and hands-off income are benefits (not the loss of control they'd be for an active investor). For these owners, giving up management is a relief, and the passive income from the units suits them.

The pros favor owners concentrated in one (or few) properties who want diversification — for whom the REIT's diversified portfolio reduces their concentration risk. They favor owners wanting liquidity — for whom the convertible units provide flexibility their illiquid property lacks. And they especially favor estate-focused owners — for whom the step-up and divisibility make the 721 a powerful wealth-transfer tool. So the pros favor owners with these goals: stepping back, diversifying, seeking liquidity, and estate planning.

These investor types — ready to transition into passive, diversified, more liquid REIT ownership, especially for estate planning — are exactly who the 721 exchange is designed for. The pros align with their goals, and the cons (loss of control, one-way nature) don't deter them (they're choosing to give up control and commit to REIT ownership). Who the pros favor — owners ready to exit active real estate, concentrated owners wanting diversification, owners seeking liquidity, and estate-focused owners — identifies the investors for whom the 721 exchange's advantages align with their goals. For these owners, the pros are compelling and the cons acceptable. Understanding who the pros favor helps you assess whether you're among them — whether the 721 exchange's advantages match your goals. If you fit these profiles, the 721 exchange's pros likely favor you.

Who should be cautious

Conversely, certain investors should be cautious about the 721 exchange, because the cons weigh heavily for them. Investors who value control should be cautious — those who want to make their own real estate decisions, manage their properties, and direct their investments will find the loss of control (becoming a passive REIT investor) a significant drawback. The 721's passivity is a con for them, not a pro.

Investors who want flexibility should be cautious — those who want to keep exchanging real property (via 1031), repositioning among properties, or retaining the option to return to direct real estate will find the one-way nature (lost 1031 eligibility) a serious drawback. For active, flexibility-valuing investors, committing to REIT ownership (irreversibly) is too costly. So control-valuing and flexibility-valuing investors should be cautious.

Investors uncomfortable with REIT dependence (relying on the REIT's management and performance, and the share-price volatility) or with the securities nature and complexity should also be cautious, ensuring they understand and accept these before proceeding. And anyone considering a 721 exchange tentatively (not ready to commit to REIT ownership) should be cautious, given the one-way nature. Who should be cautious — investors who value control or flexibility, are uncomfortable with REIT dependence or complexity, or aren't ready to commit to REIT ownership — identifies those for whom the 721 exchange's cons may outweigh its pros. For these investors, the 721 exchange may not be the right fit, and caution (careful consideration, or choosing a different strategy like the 1031) is warranted. Understanding who should be cautious helps you assess whether the cons are dealbreakers for you. If you fit these profiles, approach the 721 exchange cautiously.

Making the decision

Making the 721 exchange decision comes down to honestly assessing whether the pros align with your goals and the cons are acceptable to you. Start by clarifying your goals: do you want to exit active real estate into passive, diversified, more liquid REIT ownership (especially for estate planning)? If so, the pros likely align with your goals. Or do you want to keep controlling and exchanging direct real estate? If so, the cons (loss of control, one-way nature) likely outweigh the pros.

Then assess whether the cons are acceptable: are you ready to commit to REIT ownership (the one-way nature), give up control (passivity), depend on the REIT, and accept the conversion tax and fees? If these are acceptable given your goals, the 721 exchange may be right. If any are dealbreakers, it may not be. So the decision weighs the pros' alignment with your goals against the acceptability of the cons.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the pros of a 721 exchange?

Tax deferral (under Section 721, deferring the four-layer tax a sale would trigger), diversification (your single property becomes a stake in the REIT's diversified portfolio), liquidity (convertible units provide a path to liquidity), passive income (distributions without management), and estate planning (the step-up at death erases the gain for heirs, and units divide cleanly). These five pros — deferral, diversification, liquidity, passive income, and estate planning — make the 721 a comprehensive solution for owners ready to exit direct real estate into REIT ownership.

What are the cons of a 721 exchange?

The one-way nature (generally irreversible — you lose 1031 eligibility and can't tax-free return to direct real estate), loss of control (you become a passive investor, the REIT managing the portfolio), the conversion tax (converting units to shares triggers the deferred gain), REIT dependence (your returns depend on the REIT's management and performance, and share-price volatility), and fees/complexity (transaction and REIT costs, securities and tax complexity). These cons matter most for investors who value control and flexibility.

Is a 721 exchange worth it?

It depends on your goals. For owners ready to exit active real estate into diversified, passive, more liquid REIT ownership (especially for estate planning), the pros align with their goals and the cons (loss of control, one-way nature) are acceptable — so it's worth it. For owners who value control and flexibility (wanting to keep managing and exchanging real estate), the cons outweigh the pros — so it may not be. The 721 exchange isn't universally worth it; its value depends on whether the pros match your goals and the cons are acceptable to you.

What's the biggest drawback of a 721 exchange?

For most investors, the one-way nature — it's generally an irreversible move into REIT ownership, with the loss of 1031 eligibility (you can't tax-free return to direct real estate). This means committing to REIT ownership as an endpoint. For investors who value flexibility (wanting to keep exchanging real property), this is the most significant drawback. Closely related is the loss of control (becoming a passive REIT investor). These two — the one-way nature and loss of control — are the biggest drawbacks for control- and flexibility-valuing investors.

Who benefits most from a 721 exchange?

Owners ready to exit active real estate (tired of management, retiring, stepping back), concentrated owners wanting diversification, owners seeking liquidity, and especially estate-focused owners (for the step-up and divisibility). For these owners, the pros align with their goals, and the cons (loss of control, one-way nature) don't deter them (they're choosing to give up control and commit to REIT ownership). The 721 exchange is designed for owners transitioning into passive, diversified, more liquid REIT ownership, especially for wealth transfer — they benefit most.

Who should be cautious about a 721 exchange?

Investors who value control (wanting to make their own real estate decisions), investors who want flexibility (wanting to keep exchanging real property via 1031), investors uncomfortable with REIT dependence (relying on the REIT's performance and share-price volatility) or with the securities nature and complexity, and anyone not ready to commit to REIT ownership (given the one-way nature). For these investors, the cons may outweigh the pros, so caution (careful consideration or choosing a different strategy like the 1031) is warranted.

Does the 721 exchange's liquidity have a catch?

Yes — the liquidity comes via converting OP units to REIT shares, which is a taxable event (triggering the deferred gain). So accessing liquidity beyond the deferral has a tax cost. You can convert gradually to spread the tax, but converting always triggers gain on what you convert. And for non-traded REITs, the share liquidity itself is more limited. So the liquidity is real (especially for public REITs) but comes with the conversion tax — it's not tax-free liquidity. Understanding this catch is important when weighing the liquidity pro.

How does REIT dependence affect the decision?

After a 721 exchange, your returns and your holding's value depend on the REIT's management, performance, and (for public REITs) the share price's market volatility — you're reliant on the REIT, not your own decisions. For investors comfortable delegating to a professional REIT and accepting market exposure, this is fine (even a benefit — professional management). For investors who prefer to control their own real estate outcomes, the dependence is a con. So REIT dependence affects the decision based on your comfort with delegating to (and depending on) the REIT.

Are there fees in a 721 exchange?

Yes — there are transaction costs and the REIT's ongoing fees and expenses (management, etc.), plus the costs of the professional guidance the complex transaction requires. As with any managed investment, the REIT's fees affect your net returns. The fees and complexity are among the cons to weigh. Understanding the fee structure (the transaction costs and the REIT's ongoing expenses) is part of evaluating a 721 exchange, since fees affect your returns. Your advisor can explain the specific fees involved in a given 721 exchange opportunity.

How do I weigh the pros and cons for my situation?

Clarify your goals: if you want to exit active real estate into passive, diversified, more liquid REIT ownership (especially for estate planning), the pros likely align with your goals. If you want to keep controlling and exchanging direct real estate, the cons likely outweigh the pros. Then assess whether the cons (one-way nature, loss of control, conversion tax, REIT dependence, fees) are acceptable given your goals. Weigh the pros' alignment with your goals against the cons' acceptability — with professional guidance, given the significant, generally irreversible nature.

Is the 721 better or worse than a 1031?

Neither universally — they suit different goals. The 1031 is better for staying in direct real estate (control, flexibility, continued exchanging); the 721 is better for transitioning into REIT ownership (diversification, liquidity, passivity, estate planning). The 721's pros are advantages the 1031 lacks (diversification, liquidity), but the 721's cons (one-way, loss of control) are things the 1031 avoids. So whether the 721 is better depends on whether you want its pros (and accept its cons) or prefer the 1031's flexibility. They're different tools for different goals.

Should I get professional advice before deciding?

Yes — the 721 exchange is significant (generally irreversible), involves securities (OP units) and complex tax mechanics, and has major consequences, so you should decide with professional guidance: a financial advisor (suitability and fit), your CPA (the tax aspects), and your estate attorney (the estate-planning role). The complexity, securities nature, and irreversibility make professional advice essential. Don't make the 721 exchange decision without these professionals — their guidance ensures you weigh the pros and cons properly and decide deliberately based on your situation.

Can I do a partial 721 exchange to balance the pros and cons?

In some cases — you don't necessarily have to commit all your real estate to a 721 exchange. You could contribute some assets (gaining the pros — diversification, passivity, estate benefits — for those, accepting the cons) while keeping other property in direct real estate (retaining control and 1031 flexibility for those). This hedged approach lets you capture some of the 721's pros while keeping some direct-real-estate flexibility, balancing the trade-offs. Whether this is feasible depends on the REIT's willingness to accept your contribution and your overall plan, which you'd discuss with your advisors.

How do fees compare to a 1031 exchange?

Both have costs, but of different kinds. A 1031 has transaction costs (the sale and purchase of real estate, qualified intermediary fees) and the ongoing costs of owning and managing the property yourself. A 721 exchange has the transaction costs of the contribution plus the REIT's ongoing fees and expenses (management, etc.), which are built into the REIT's structure. So with a 721 you're paying for professional management (via the REIT's fees), whereas with a 1031 you manage yourself (saving management fees but doing the work). Weigh the REIT's fees against the value of professional management and the other 721 benefits.
