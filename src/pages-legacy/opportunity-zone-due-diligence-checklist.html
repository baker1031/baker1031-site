Opportunity Zone Due Diligence Checklist

Back to All Insights
Opportunity Zone Funds Baker 1031 Research  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  16 min read

Before committing capital to a Qualified Opportunity Fund for a decade, disciplined due diligence is essential. This practical checklist walks through the five areas to evaluate — vet the sponsor, analyze the project and market, confirm OZ compliance, review fees and promote, and assess the exit strategy — so you can evaluate a QOF with clear eyes. Educational only, not investment advice.

Opportunity Zone investing commits your capital for a long time — ideally ten years or more — into an illiquid, often development-stage investment, so the quality of your due diligence matters enormously. The tax benefits are only valuable if the underlying investment performs, which means evaluating a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF) the way you would any serious private real-estate investment: scrutinizing the sponsor, the project and market, the OZ compliance, the fees and promote, and the exit strategy. A disciplined, checklist-driven process helps you separate sound funds from weak ones and avoid letting the tax incentive drive you into a poor investment. This guide is a practical due diligence checklist for evaluating an OZ fund, organized into five areas: vet the sponsor, analyze the project and market, confirm OZ compliance, review fees and promote, and assess the exit strategy. Use it as a framework for your own diligence and your conversations with the fund and your advisors. This is educational information, not investment advice — and OZ rules are time-sensitive and evolving, so verify the current rules with your tax advisor.

Vet the sponsor

Vetting the sponsor is the foundation of OZ due diligence, because the sponsor's ability to execute largely determines the investment's success — especially for development funds. Examine the sponsor's experience (years in the business, depth of the team), their track record (prior projects, realized returns, and how they navigated downturns), and their reputation (references, litigation history, and standing in the industry). A capable, experienced, reputable sponsor reduces execution and sponsor risk.

Dig into the specifics: Has the sponsor completed similar projects (same property type, similar scale, similar markets)? What were the outcomes — did they deliver on time and on budget, and did investors earn the projected returns? How are they capitalized and staffed to execute this project? Have they managed OZ funds specifically, with the compliance discipline the program requires? A sponsor with a proven, relevant track record and clean reputation is far preferable to an inexperienced or troubled one.

So vetting the sponsor — their experience, track record, and reputation — is the first and most important diligence step. Analyze the project & market

Analyzing the project and market tests whether the underlying investment is sound on its own merits. For the project, examine the business plan (what's being built or improved, the budget, the timeline, the assumptions), the property type and strategy (ground-up development, renovation, or operating asset), and the financial projections (the underwriting, the return assumptions, and how realistic they are). A sound project has a credible plan, reasonable assumptions, and a margin for error.

For the market, examine the location fundamentals — the demand drivers (population and job growth, demographics), the supply picture (competing projects, absorption), and the broader economic trends in the area. An OZ designation alone doesn't make a location good; you want genuine demand and growth prospects independent of the tax incentive. Be wary of projects that rely on the OZ designation rather than real market strength. So scrutinize whether the project and its market would be attractive even without the tax benefits.

Confirm OZ compliance

Confirming OZ compliance ensures the fund actually qualifies for (and will maintain) the program's benefits — without compliance, the tax advantages can be lost. Key items to confirm include: the fund holds qualifying opportunity zone property (qualifying business property in a designated zone), it meets the substantial improvement requirement where applicable (for existing buildings, generally doubling the basis of improvements within the required period), and it satisfies the 90% asset test (a QOF must hold at least 90% of its assets in qualified OZ property, measured periodically).

Also confirm the fund avoids prohibited 'sin businesses' (certain excluded businesses like golf courses, country clubs, massage parlors, gambling facilities, and liquor stores, which don't qualify), and that it has a sound working-capital plan (the working-capital safe harbor lets a fund hold cash for a development plan if structured properly, with a written plan and schedule). These compliance elements are technical, so confirm the fund and its counsel have addressed them — a fund that mishandles compliance can jeopardize the benefits.

Review fees & promote

Reviewing fees and the promote reveals how much of the investment's return goes to the sponsor versus you. Examine the fee structure — acquisition fees, asset-management fees, development fees, disposition fees, and any other charges — and understand how they're calculated and when they're paid. High or layered fees drag on your net return, so you want a reasonable, transparent fee structure aligned with the value the sponsor provides.

Pay particular attention to the promote (carried interest) — the share of profits the sponsor earns above a return threshold (the hurdle or preferred return). Understand the preferred return (the return investors receive before the sponsor shares in profits), the promote percentage, and any catch-up or waterfall tiers. A well-structured promote aligns the sponsor's incentives with yours (they earn more when you do well), while an aggressive promote or a low/no hurdle can shift too much upside to the sponsor. So evaluate whether the fees and promote are reasonable and aligned.

Assess exit strategy

Assessing the exit strategy clarifies how and when you'll get your capital back — critical for an illiquid, long-hold OZ investment. Because the tax-free exclusion requires a 10-year hold, the exit is typically structured around the 10-year mark, so examine the fund's planned exit: will it sell the assets, refinance to return capital, or offer another liquidity mechanism, and on what timeline? Understand whether the fund's structure and term align with the 10-year hold you need for the exclusion.

Consider the realism and flexibility of the exit plan: Does the sponsor have a credible path to sell or recapitalize the assets at the 10-year mark? What happens if market conditions are poor at that time — is there flexibility to extend or wait? Is there any interim liquidity (generally limited or none in OZ funds), and what are the consequences of an early exit (forfeiting the exclusion, a possible inclusion event)? A clear, realistic, 10-year-aligned exit plan is important; a vague or misaligned one is a red flag.

So assessing the exit strategy — the planned exit, its timeline, and its alignment with the 10-year hold — clarifies how you'll realize the investment. Putting the checklist to work

Putting the checklist to work means applying all five areas — sponsor, project and market, compliance, fees and promote, and exit — together, since a fund must hold up across the board, not just on one dimension. A great sponsor with a poor market, or a sound project with abusive fees, or a compliant fund with no realistic exit, each falls short. So weigh the areas collectively to form an overall judgment about the fund.

Use the checklist to structure your questions to the fund and sponsor, to organize the documents you review (the PPM, financials, projections, and compliance materials), and to coordinate with your advisors — your CPA on the tax/compliance points, your attorney on the legal terms, and your financial advisor on suitability and fit. What is the 90% asset test?

The 90% asset test is a core QOF compliance requirement: a Qualified Opportunity Fund must hold at least 90% of its assets in qualified opportunity zone property, measured at testing dates (generally semi-annually and at year-end). The test ensures the fund actually deploys its capital into qualifying OZ investments rather than holding it outside the program. A fund that fails the 90% test can face penalties, so funds manage their holdings (often using the working-capital safe harbor for development cash) to satisfy it. When evaluating a fund, confirm it has a plan and track record for meeting the 90% test, since a compliance failure can affect the benefits. So the 90% test is a key compliance item to verify — the fund must hold at least 90% of its assets in qualified OZ property at the testing dates. This is technical, so have your CPA review the fund's compliance approach and verify the current rules, which are evolving.

What are 'sin businesses' that don't qualify for OZ benefits?

Certain businesses are specifically excluded from OZ benefits — commonly called 'sin businesses.' These generally include golf courses, country clubs, massage parlors, hot tub facilities, suntan facilities, gambling/racetrack facilities, and stores whose principal business is selling alcoholic beverages for off-premises consumption (liquor stores). A QOF's qualified opportunity zone business generally cannot be one of these excluded businesses. So when vetting a fund, confirm its underlying businesses or properties aren't in an excluded category, since that would disqualify them from the OZ benefits. This is usually straightforward for typical real-estate development funds (apartments, commercial, mixed-use), but it's worth confirming. So 'sin businesses' are the statutorily excluded categories that don't qualify for OZ treatment — verify the fund avoids them. What are red flags in an OZ fund?

Common red flags include: a sponsor with little relevant experience, a thin or troubled track record, or litigation concerns; a project in a weak market that leans on the OZ designation rather than real demand; unrealistic or aggressive financial projections; high, layered, or opaque fees and an aggressive promote with little or no preferred return; compliance gaps or a vague approach to the qualifying-property, substantial-improvement, 90%-test, or working-capital requirements; and a vague, unrealistic, or misaligned exit strategy that doesn't fit the 10-year hold. Pressure to invest quickly, a lack of transparency, or an over-emphasis on the tax benefits (rather than the investment merits) are also warning signs. So watch for weakness in any of the five diligence areas, plus general red flags like opacity and pressure. So a careful checklist-driven review helps surface these red flags before you commit. This is educational information — discuss any concerns with your advisors, who can help you evaluate the specific fund.

Does OZ compliance affect my tax benefits?

Yes — OZ compliance is what makes (and keeps) the tax benefits available. The benefits depend on the fund qualifying as a QOF (self-certifying on Form 8996), holding qualifying opportunity zone property, meeting the substantial-improvement requirement where applicable, satisfying the 90% asset test, avoiding prohibited businesses, and properly using the working-capital safe harbor. If a fund mishandles these requirements, the tax benefits can be reduced or lost, and there can be penalties. So confirming the fund's compliance approach is an important part of due diligence — a fund that doesn't manage compliance well puts your benefits at risk. This is why having your CPA or attorney review the fund's compliance is prudent. So yes, compliance directly affects your benefits, which is why it's a core checklist area. How long does OZ due diligence take?

It varies, but you should allow enough time to do it properly — rushing diligence on a decade-long, illiquid commitment is a mistake. Reviewing the sponsor, project and market, OZ compliance, fees and promote, and exit strategy, and coordinating with your CPA, attorney, and financial advisor, typically takes at least several weeks, depending on the fund's complexity and how responsive the sponsor is with documents. The 180-day window to invest your gain provides a deadline, but you should still build in time for thorough review rather than committing under time pressure near the deadline — which is why planning your QOF investment in advance (identifying candidate funds early) is valuable. So don't compress diligence into the last few days before your deadline; start early so you can evaluate the fund carefully across all five areas and consult your advisors. A disciplined, unhurried process leads to a better-informed decision. So allow ample time for OZ due diligence, and plan ahead so the 180-day clock doesn't force a rushed commitment. Glossary

Due Diligence
The disciplined evaluation of a fund before investing.
Sponsor

Track Record

Location Fundamentals
The market's demand, supply, and economic trends.
Business Plan

Qualifying OZ Property
