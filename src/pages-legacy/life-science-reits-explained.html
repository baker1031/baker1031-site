What Life Science REITs Own

Life science REITs own laboratory and research real estate — purpose-built buildings designed for scientific work rather than ordinary office use. These are life-science buildings leased to biotechnology companies, pharmaceutical firms, and research organizations that need wet labs, dry labs, and specialized research space. The defining feature is that the buildings are engineered for science: they include advanced HVAC and ventilation systems, redundant power, specialized plumbing for lab gases and water, vibration control for sensitive instruments, and higher ceiling heights and floor loads than a typical office. This makes lab space fundamentally different from — and more expensive to build than — conventional office space.

Geographically, life science real estate is highly clustered. Most of it sits in a handful of innovation hubs where research universities, talent, venture capital, and established companies congregate — notably the Boston and Cambridge area, the San Francisco Bay Area, and San Diego, along with a few other emerging clusters. This clustering exists because life-science companies want to be near the talent, collaborators, and capital that drive the industry, so the real estate concentrates where the ecosystem is strongest. As a result, a life science REIT's portfolio is often concentrated in these markets, and the value of its buildings is tied to the health of those specific clusters.

So a life science REIT owns purpose-built lab and research buildings — engineered for science and clustered in a few innovation hubs — leased to biotech, pharma, and research tenants. Biotech & Pharma Demand

Demand for life science real estate is driven by the growth of biotech and pharmaceutical research and development. As biotechnology and pharma companies expand their pipelines, scale up research, and form new ventures, they need more laboratory and research space to house scientists, instruments, and experiments. The long-run drivers are powerful: an aging population, advances in genomics and drug discovery, and steady investment in life sciences all tend to support demand for lab space over time, which is part of what positions the sector among growth REITs investing for appreciation. When the industry is growing and well-funded, demand for purpose-built lab space in the major clusters can be robust.

This demand has a particular character. Life-science tenants range from large, creditworthy pharmaceutical companies and established biotechs to small, early-stage startups still developing their first products — so tenant credit and stage vary widely across a portfolio. Larger tenants offer stronger credit and stability; smaller, earlier-stage tenants offer growth but more risk, since many are pre-revenue and depend on outside funding. The clustering effect reinforces demand: companies want to be where the talent, universities, and collaborators are, so the established hubs tend to attract a disproportionate share of activity. So demand is real and structurally supported, but its strength varies with the health and funding of the biotech and pharma industry.

Specialized Lab Space

The specialized nature of lab space is what gives life science REITs much of their economic appeal. Purpose-built laboratories require advanced infrastructure — sophisticated HVAC and ventilation to handle fume hoods and air exchanges, redundant and high-capacity power, specialized gas and water systems, vibration isolation for sensitive equipment, and structural features ordinary offices lack. This infrastructure is expensive to build and difficult to retrofit into conventional space, which limits supply and raises the replacement cost of existing lab buildings. As a result, well-located, modern lab space tends to command higher rents than comparable office space.

This specialization also makes tenants sticky. Once a biotech or pharma company has built out a lab — installing equipment, validating clean rooms, and configuring the space for its research — moving is costly and disruptive, both financially and to ongoing experiments. That high cost of relocation gives tenants a strong incentive to stay and renew, which can support occupancy and rent stability for the landlord. The combination of hard-to-replicate space, limited supply, premium rents, and sticky tenants is the core investment case for the sector. The trade-off is that highly specialized space can be harder to re-lease to a non-lab tenant if a life-science tenant leaves, since not every business needs (or wants to pay for) lab infrastructure.

So specialized lab space — costly to build, hard to replicate, commanding premium rents, and retaining sticky tenants — is the economic heart of life science REITs, with re-leasing the main caveat. Funding-Cycle Risk

The defining risk of life science REITs is funding-cycle risk. Tenant demand for lab space is tied closely to the availability of biotech venture capital and research funding. When venture capital is abundant and the biotech IPO and financing markets are open, startups raise money, expand, and lease more lab space, and demand is strong. But when funding tightens — during a biotech downturn, a pullback in venture capital, or a difficult financing environment — early-stage and pre-revenue tenants may slow expansion, cut space, or fail outright, which reduces demand and can push vacancy higher. Because so many life-science tenants depend on outside capital rather than current profits, the sector's demand is unusually sensitive to funding conditions.

This funding sensitivity is what makes life science REITs more cyclical than their specialized, high-rent space might suggest. A wave of new lab supply delivered into a softening funding environment can be especially difficult, because rising vacancy and falling demand can pressure rents and occupancy at the same time. The risk is concentrated among smaller, earlier-stage tenants; larger, well-capitalized pharma and established biotech tenants are more resilient through downturns. So a portfolio's exposure to funding-cycle risk depends heavily on its tenant mix — the more it leans on early-stage, funding-dependent tenants and on new, speculative supply, the more sensitive it is to the biotech funding cycle.

So funding-cycle risk — demand tied to biotech venture and research funding, with downturns reducing demand and raising vacancy, concentrated in early-stage tenants — is the central vulnerability of life science REITs. Supply Pipeline and Market Concentration

Beyond funding, the supply pipeline is a crucial factor for life science REITs, because lab-space economics depend on the balance between demand and new construction. When developers race to build new lab buildings in popular clusters — often in response to a period of strong demand — a large pipeline of new supply can come online over several years. If that supply arrives while demand is still strong, it may be absorbed; but if it lands during a funding downturn, the combination of rising vacancy and abundant new space can pressure rents and occupancy across a market. So tracking the construction pipeline in each key cluster is essential to understanding where rents and vacancy may be headed.

Market concentration compounds this. Because life science real estate is concentrated in a few hubs, a life science REIT's fortunes are tied to the health of those specific markets — their funding climate, talent pools, and supply pipelines. A REIT heavily concentrated in a single cluster is more exposed to that market's supply-and-demand swings than one diversified across several hubs. Some REITs also focus on the strongest sub-markets and on properties anchored by larger, creditworthy tenants, which can cushion against cluster-specific shocks. So both the supply pipeline and the degree of market concentration shape a life science REIT's risk, alongside the funding cycle and tenant mix.

So the supply pipeline and market concentration — the pace of new lab construction in each hub and how concentrated a REIT is in particular clusters — are key factors layered on top of funding-cycle risk. Evaluating Life Science REITs

Evaluating a life science REIT begins with location and cluster quality, because the sector is so geographically concentrated. Which innovation hubs is the REIT in, how deep and durable is each cluster's ecosystem of talent, universities, and capital, and how diversified is the portfolio across markets? A REIT anchored in strong, established clusters with diversified exposure is generally better positioned than one concentrated in a single, less-proven market. Next, assess tenant credit and stage: what share of rent comes from large, creditworthy pharma and established biotech versus small, early-stage, funding-dependent startups? A tenant base weighted toward stronger credits is more resilient through funding downturns.

Then examine the supply pipeline in each key market — how much new lab space is being delivered, and how does it compare with demand — since oversupply into a soft funding environment is a major risk. Consider the REIT's leverage and balance sheet, the quality and modernity of its buildings, lease terms and tenant retention, and how exposed it is to funding-cycle risk through its tenant mix. Finally, treat demand and outlook as general and non-promissory: biotech funding is cyclical, distributions aren't guaranteed, and share prices fluctuate. Past performance doesn't guarantee future results, and current cluster conditions, funding climate, and supply pipelines should be verified independently rather than assumed from a strong recent period.

So evaluating a life science REIT means weighing cluster and location quality, tenant credit and stage, the supply pipeline, leverage, building quality, and funding-cycle exposure. How is lab space different from office space?

Lab space differs fundamentally from office space in design, cost, and economics. A laboratory is engineered for science: it needs advanced HVAC and ventilation to handle fume hoods and frequent air exchanges, redundant high-capacity power, specialized plumbing for lab gases and water, vibration isolation for sensitive instruments, and stronger floors and higher ceilings than a typical office. This infrastructure makes lab space far more expensive to build and difficult to retrofit into conventional space, which limits supply and supports higher rents than comparable office space. Lab space also makes tenants sticky — once a company builds out and validates a lab, moving is costly and disruptive to research, so tenants tend to stay and renew. By contrast, office space is cheaper to build, more interchangeable, and easier to re-lease to a wide range of tenants. The trade-off for lab space is that it can be harder to repurpose for non-lab users. So lab space is specialized, costly, premium-rent, and sticky, where office space is general-purpose and flexible.

How does the supply pipeline affect life science REITs?

The supply pipeline — the amount of new lab space under construction and being delivered in each market — is a crucial factor for life science REITs, because lab-space economics depend on the balance between demand and new supply. When developers build aggressively in popular clusters, often after a period of strong demand, a large pipeline can come online over several years. If that new space is delivered while demand remains strong, it may be absorbed without much trouble. How do I evaluate the tenants of a life science REIT?

Start by assessing tenant credit and development stage, because life-science tenants range widely. Large, creditworthy pharmaceutical companies and established, well-funded biotechs offer stronger credit and stability, while small, early-stage startups offer growth but more risk, since many are pre-revenue and depend on outside funding. Look at what share of the REIT's rent comes from each group — a portfolio weighted toward stronger credits is more resilient through funding downturns, while one leaning on early-stage tenants is more exposed to funding-cycle risk. Examine lease terms and tenant retention, since sticky, long-term tenants in well-located buildings support stable income. Consider the diversity of the tenant base across companies, stages, and clusters, so that trouble at any one tenant or in any one market has a limited effect. Finally, weigh how the tenant mix interacts with the supply pipeline and funding climate in each cluster. So evaluating life-science-REIT tenants means weighing credit, stage, lease terms, retention, and diversification together, with special attention to funding-dependent tenants.

Are life science REITs cyclical?

Yes — life science REITs are more cyclical than their specialized, high-rent space might suggest, primarily because of funding-cycle risk. Tenant demand for lab space is tied to biotech venture capital and research funding, and many life-science tenants, especially early-stage ones, depend on outside capital rather than current profits. So when funding is abundant, demand for lab space is strong; when funding tightens during a biotech downturn, demand can soften, early-stage tenants can contract or fail, and vacancy can rise. The cyclicality is amplified when new lab supply is delivered into a weakening funding environment, since rising vacancy and abundant supply can pressure rents and occupancy at once. That said, the sector also has structural, long-run demand drivers — an aging population and advances in biotech and pharma research — that support it over time, and larger, well-capitalized tenants are more resilient through cycles. So life science REITs combine durable long-term demand with real short-term cyclicality tied to biotech funding, and a portfolio's tenant mix and supply exposure determine how cyclical it actually is.

How do interest rates affect life science REITs?

Like most REITs, life science REITs are sensitive to interest rates. Higher rates can pressure REIT share prices because income-oriented REITs compete with bonds for investors, so when yields on bonds rise, the relative appeal of a REIT's distribution can decline even if its rents are unchanged. Higher borrowing costs also make it more expensive for REITs to finance acquisitions and development, which can slow growth and weigh on the economics of new lab projects. For life science REITs specifically, interest rates can interact with the sector's funding-cycle risk: tighter financial conditions often coincide with a harder biotech funding environment, which can soften tenant demand at the same time that the REIT's own cost of capital rises. The specialized, hard-to-replicate nature of lab space and long-term leases provide some insulation, but share prices can still move with rates. So when evaluating a life science REIT, consider its leverage, debt maturities, and how rate conditions may interact with the biotech funding climate. Why is lab space hard to replace if a tenant leaves?

Lab space can be harder to re-lease than ordinary commercial real estate if a life-science tenant departs, and the reason is its specialization. A laboratory is purpose-built with advanced HVAC and ventilation, redundant high-capacity power, specialized gas and water systems, vibration isolation, and other infrastructure that conventional businesses don't need and may not want to pay for. So while the specialized buildout makes existing lab tenants sticky — relocating is costly and disruptive — it also narrows the pool of potential replacement tenants to other lab users, who tend to cluster in the same innovation hubs. In a strong market with healthy biotech demand, a well-located lab building can usually find a new lab tenant; but in a funding downturn, with fewer expanding tenants and rising vacancy, re-leasing specialized space can take longer and require concessions. This re-leasing dynamic is part of why location and cluster quality matter so much: a building in a deep, well-funded hub has a larger pool of potential lab tenants than one in a thin or fading market. So specialization cuts both ways — it retains tenants but can complicate re-leasing when one leaves.
