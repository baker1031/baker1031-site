Opportunity Zone Deadlines and Program Timeline

Back to All Insights
Opportunity Zone Funds Baker 1031 Research  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  16 min read

Opportunity Zone investing involves several key dates and deadlines — the 180-day investment window, the deferred-gain recognition date, and program designation and sunset dates. This guide lays out the key dates, when deferred gain is recognized, the program's permanence and new-map transition, how timing affects your benefits, and how to stay current on the evolving rules.

Opportunity Zone investing is governed by a set of important dates and deadlines — and because the program has recently changed significantly (made permanent, with new rules and zones under the 2025 OZ 2.0 legislation), keeping track of the timeline is more important than ever. Key dates include the 180-day window to invest a gain, the date the deferred gain is recognized (which differs between the original and revised program), and the program's designation and transition dates (the new zone map and the overlap period). Missing a deadline (like the 180-day window) can forfeit benefits, and the timing affects your tax outcomes. This guide lays out the key Opportunity Zone dates, when deferred gain is recognized, the program's permanence and transitions, how timing affects your benefits, and how to stay current. Critically, the rules are time-sensitive and evolving — verify the current law with your tax advisor.

Key Opportunity Zone dates

Several key dates govern Opportunity Zone investing. The 180-day investment window — you have 180 days from realizing a capital gain to invest it into a QOF (with flexible start dates for pass-through gains). This is the deadline to act to earn the OZ benefits for a gain.

The deferred-gain recognition date — the date your deferred original gain is recognized (taxed): under the original program (OZ 1.0), December 31, 2026 (a fixed date); under OZ 2.0 (for post-2026 investments), a rolling 5 years from investing. The 10-year mark — the point, 10 years after investing, at which you qualify for the tax-free exclusion of the OZ investment's appreciation.

And program designation dates — the current zone map (in effect, with designations running through the end of 2028) and the new zone map (effective January 1, 2027, under the permanent program). So the key dates include the 180-day window, the recognition date, the 10-year mark, and the zone-designation dates. When deferred gain is recognized

The deferred-gain recognition date is a critical timing point that differs between the program versions. Under the original program (OZ 1.0), all deferred gains are recognized on a fixed date of December 31, 2026. So OZ 1.0 investors recognize (and pay tax on) their deferred original gain at the end of 2026, regardless of when they invested. This fixed date is now imminent (from the perspective of mid-2026).

Under the revised program (OZ 2.0, for investments made after December 31, 2026), the deferral is a rolling 5 years from the investment date — the deferred gain is recognized 5 years after you invest (or upon an earlier inclusion event). So OZ 2.0 investments have an individualized recognition date (5 years out), rather than a single fixed date.

So when your deferred gain is recognized depends on the program version: a fixed December 31, 2026 for OZ 1.0, or a rolling 5 years for OZ 2.0. Program permanence & transitions

The original Opportunity Zone program had a sunset, but the 2025 legislation made the program permanent — a major change. Under the original program (OZ 1.0), the incentive was temporary (with the deferral recognition date of December 31, 2026, and zone designations set to expire), creating a sunset that loomed over the program.

The 2025 One Big Beautiful Bill Act, which ushered in OZ 2.0, made the Opportunity Zone incentive permanent, establishing a recurring cycle of zone designations (updating the zone map roughly every 10 years) — eliminating the prior sunset. So the program is now a permanent feature, not a temporary one set to expire.

The transition involves a new zone map (effective January 1, 2027, with governors nominating from mid-2026) overlapping the current map (which runs through the end of 2028) for a period. So the program moved from a sunsetting temporary incentive to a permanent one, with a zone-map transition underway. How timing affects your benefits

Timing affects your OZ benefits in several ways. The 180-day window — missing it forfeits the OZ benefits for a gain (you must invest within the window). So meeting the investment deadline is essential to earning any benefits.

The recognition date — when your deferred gain is recognized (and taxed) affects your tax planning (you'll owe the tax then, so plan for the liability). For OZ 1.0 investors, the December 31, 2026 date means a near-term tax bill on the deferred gain. The 10-year mark — you must hold from your investment date to the 10-year mark to earn the tax-free exclusion (timing your hold to reach 10 years is essential to the marquee benefit).

And the program version (OZ 1.0 vs. OZ 2.0) — the timing of your investment (before or after December 31, 2026) determines which rules apply (the recognition timing, the available benefits). So timing affects whether you get benefits (the 180-day window), when you owe tax (the recognition date), whether you get the exclusion (the 10-year hold), and which rules apply (the program version). Does the Opportunity Zone program have a sunset?

Not anymore for the core incentive — the original program (OZ 1.0) was temporary (with a December 31, 2026 recognition date and expiring zone designations, creating a sunset), but the 2025 One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OZ 2.0) made the Opportunity Zone incentive permanent, establishing a recurring cycle of zone designations (updating the map roughly every 10 years). So the program is now a permanent feature, not a temporary one set to expire — the prior sunset is gone. However, specific elements still have dates (the OZ 1.0 recognition date of December 31, 2026, the new map effective January 1, 2027, the current map running through 2028). So while the program is now permanent, particular dates and transitions still apply — verify the current rules, as the program has fundamentally changed from sunsetting to permanent.

What is the new Opportunity Zone map timeline?

Under the permanent program (OZ 2.0), a new cycle of zone designations is underway: governors nominate new tracts (from mid-2026), the Treasury certifies them, and the new map of zones takes effect January 1, 2027, lasting about 10 years (with decennial updates). The current map remains in effect through the end of 2028, overlapping the new map for a period. So the zone-map timeline involves the current map (through 2028) and the new map (effective January 1, 2027), with an overlap. This means the designated zones are transitioning — when researching or investing, confirm which zones are designated for your timeframe on the current, authoritative map. So the new-map timeline is: nominations from mid-2026, new map effective January 1, 2027, current map through 2028 (overlap), then decennial updates. What happens if I miss the 180-day window?

You forfeit the OZ benefits for that gain — if you don't invest the gain into a QOF within the 180-day window, it isn't eligible for the OZ deferral or the 10-year exclusion, so you'll owe the capital-gains tax on it (as a normal taxable gain) with no OZ benefit. There's generally no extension for missing the window (it's a firm deadline), though the flexible start dates for pass-through gains can effectively give more time (if applicable). So missing the 180-day window means losing the opportunity to earn the OZ benefits for that gain. This makes tracking the deadline critical. So identify your gains and deadlines early, and invest within the window to preserve the benefits. The 180-day window is one of the most important deadlines in OZ investing — missing it is costly and generally irreversible for that gain.

Are the OZ deadlines the same as 1031 deadlines?

No — they're different. A 1031 exchange has two deadlines: 45 days to identify replacement property and 180 days to close. The OZ has a single investment deadline: 180 days to invest the gain into a QOF (no separate identification requirement, and with flexible start dates for pass-through gains). The OZ also has the recognition date (when the deferred gain is taxed) and the 10-year mark (for the exclusion), which the 1031 doesn't have (the 1031 defers indefinitely with the step-up at death). So the OZ and 1031 timelines differ — the OZ's 180-day single window and its recognition/10-year dates versus the 1031's 45/180-day dual deadlines and indefinite deferral. So don't confuse them; each strategy has its own distinct timeline and deadlines. Track the correct deadlines for whichever strategy you're using.

If I invest in a QOF in 2026, am I under OZ 1.0 or OZ 2.0?

Generally, the program version depends on when you invest relative to the December 31, 2026 dividing line — investments made under the original program (through 2026) follow OZ 1.0 rules (including the December 31, 2026 deferred-gain recognition date), while investments made after December 31, 2026 follow the OZ 2.0 rules (the rolling 5-year deferral, new zones). So a 2026 investment would generally fall under the original program's framework for the deferral timing. However, the transition mechanics are technical and the rules are being implemented, so the precise treatment of a late-2026 investment should be confirmed with your CPA. So your program version turns on the timing of your investment around the end of 2026 — verify the specifics for your situation, as this affects your recognition date and applicable rules. The transition period makes professional confirmation especially important for investments near the boundary.

Will my 10-year clock be affected by the program changes?

Generally, your 10-year holding period (for the tax-free exclusion) runs from your investment date, and the 10-year exclusion has been preserved under the permanent program — so the program changes don't reset or shorten your 10-year clock; it continues from when you invested. So if you invested and are holding toward the 10-year mark, you keep counting from your investment date toward the exclusion. The permanence of the program (OZ 2.0) supports the continued availability of the 10-year exclusion. However, given the transition, confirm the specifics with your CPA, especially regarding any timing cutoffs for making the basis-step-up election. So your 10-year clock generally continues unaffected by the program changes, running from your investment date — but verify the current rules for your situation, as the transition mechanics are technical and worth confirming to ensure your exclusion is preserved as expected.
