721 Exchange Closing Documents Explained

Back to All Insights
721 Exchange Baker 1031 Research  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  16 min read

A 721 exchange involves several key closing documents that establish your OP units, your rights as a partner, your tax protection, and your conversion rights. This guide explains the main documents — the contribution agreement, tax protection agreement, partnership agreement, and registration rights agreement — what each does, why it matters, and how to review them.

A 721 exchange is documented through several important legal agreements that establish the terms of your transition into REIT ownership. Understanding these closing documents is essential, because they define what you receive (the OP units), your rights as a partner, the protections for your deferred gain (the tax protection agreement), and your ability to convert units to shares (the registration rights). Because the 721 exchange is a significant, generally irreversible transaction, the documents matter — they govern your ownership for the long term, so reviewing them carefully (with your attorney) is important. This guide explains the main 721 exchange closing documents — the contribution agreement, tax protection agreement, partnership agreement, and registration rights agreement — what each does, why each matters, and how to review them.

The key documents overview

A 721 exchange involves several key documents, each governing a different aspect of the transaction and your resulting ownership. The main documents are the contribution agreement (effecting the contribution of your property for OP units), the tax protection agreement (protecting your deferred gain), the partnership agreement (governing your rights as a partner), and often a registration rights agreement (governing your ability to convert units to shares and have them registered for sale).

Together, these documents establish the complete framework of your 721 exchange — what you contribute and receive (contribution agreement), your tax protection (tax protection agreement), your rights as a unit holder (partnership agreement), and your conversion/liquidity rights (registration rights). So understanding each document's role gives you a complete picture of the transaction's terms.

Because these documents define your ownership and rights for the long term (the 721 exchange is generally permanent), they're important to understand and review. The documents aren't mere formalities; they govern your investment. The contribution agreement

The contribution agreement is the foundational document that effects the 721 exchange — the contribution of your property to the operating partnership in exchange for OP units. It sets out the core terms: the property you're contributing — which must be property that qualifies for the contribution — its agreed value, the number of OP units you'll receive, and the mechanics of the transfer (the Section 721 contribution).

The contribution agreement establishes the economic terms of your exchange — what you give (your property) and what you get (the units) — so it's central. It also typically includes representations and warranties (about the property and the parties), the closing conditions, and other transactional terms. So the contribution agreement is the primary transactional document, defining the exchange itself.

Reviewing the contribution agreement is important to confirm the terms (especially the property value and units received, which determine your economic outcome) are as agreed and to understand the representations and conditions. So the contribution agreement is the key document for the transaction's core terms. The tax protection agreement

The tax protection agreement is a crucial document that protects your deferred gain from being triggered prematurely. As discussed in our Section 721 guide, if the operating partnership sells your contributed property in a taxable way, your deferred (built-in) gain could be triggered. The tax protection agreement addresses this by having the REIT/partnership agree to protect you — typically by not selling the contributed property in a taxable way for a period, or compensating you if a triggering sale occurs.

The tax protection agreement's terms — the protected period (how long the protection lasts), the form of protection (a covenant not to sell, a make-whole payment, debt maintenance, etc.), and the specifics — determine how well your deferred gain is protected. So this document is important for the security of your deferral. A strong tax protection agreement (longer period, robust protection) better safeguards your deferral; a weak one leaves you more exposed.

Reviewing and negotiating the tax protection agreement (with your attorney) is important, since it directly affects your deferral's security. The partnership agreement

The partnership agreement (the operating partnership's governing document) defines your rights and obligations as a partner (OP unit holder). It governs the operating partnership — how it's managed (typically by the REIT as general partner), the rights of the limited partners (you), the distributions, the conversion rights (units to shares), and other terms of being a partner.

As an OP unit holder, you become subject to the partnership agreement, so it defines your ownership interest's terms — your distribution rights, your conversion rights, your (limited) governance rights, and the partnership's operation. So understanding the partnership agreement clarifies what your OP units entitle you to and how the partnership works. It's the document governing your ongoing ownership.

Key things to understand in the partnership agreement include the distribution provisions (how distributions are determined and paid), the conversion provisions (how and when you can convert units to shares, including the lock-up), and your rights and protections as a limited partner. So the partnership agreement is the document governing your ongoing role as a unit holder. The registration rights agreement

A registration rights agreement (common for exchanges involving publicly-traded REITs) governs your ability to have your converted shares registered for sale. When you convert OP units to REIT shares (in a public REIT), the shares may need to be registered with the SEC for you to sell them freely on the market. The registration rights agreement gives you the right to have your shares registered, enabling their sale.

This document matters for your liquidity — it affects your ability to sell the REIT shares you convert into. Without registration rights, your converted shares might be restricted (harder to sell); with them, you have a path to having the shares registered for free sale. So the registration rights agreement supports the liquidity aspect of your OP units (the ability to ultimately sell the converted shares).

The terms of the registration rights (when and how you can demand or participate in registration) affect your liquidity path. So understanding the registration rights (for a traded-REIT exchange) is part of understanding your liquidity. (For non-traded REITs, liquidity works through redemption programs rather than registered share sales, so registration rights may be less central.)
